Lesson 2 | Teacher guidance Non-financial Aims and Objectives/Exam Skills 2

  • Worksheets will need to be printed for students in advance of the lesson.
  • While all care is taken to ensure resource content is in line with cultural sensitivities, please be aware that it should be reviewed before sharing with students.

Financial or non-financial objectives?

  • This activity is intended to recap the financial objectives material from Lesson 1 and introduce non-financial objectives as the first topic for today’s lesson.
  • Distribute Worksheet 1 and ask students to complete question 1. They should tick the relevant column for each objective.
  • Go through the objectives as a class, calling on individual students to say which category they think each one belongs to.
  • Ask for reasons for any objective which a student has categorised differently from the suggested answers below – some of these are subjective, so allowing students to justify their ideas allows them to demonstrate a higher-order skill.
  • Ask students to complete question 2. They should write an objective of their own for each of the categories on the worksheet.
  • Explain that the majority of objectives contain some element that is non-financial, and those objectives will be the main focus of today’s lesson.
  • If time permits, ask volunteers to suggest their own ideas for financial objectives. Use these suggestions to refer back to Lesson 1. Ask students: Can anyone tell me whether these objectives relate to profit, sales, market share, survival or financial security?

Non-financial aims and objectives

  • Ask students: Think about why someone might want to run their own business rather than work for someone else. Focus on non-financial reasons, as these are our focus for today.
  • Ask volunteers to share suggestions. Then show Slide 1 of Slideshow: Freelance or employed?, which includes some possible answers.
  • Explain that many of the student suggestions can fit into three categories of (non-financial) objective that can be described as:
    • personal satisfaction
    • challenge
    • independence and control.
  • Show Slide 2 of the slideshow and ask students to: Sort the suggestions into one or more of those categories. Give students 2 minutes to do this.
  • If any students finish early, ask them whether they can think of any businesses/people who have started companies for those reasons and, if so, to describe the company and the type of non-financial objective they think the owner had (they may have family members who have done so or know of commercial examples).
    (Possible answers for the sorting activity could include:
    • Personal satisfaction – being creative, having an impact on the world, gain prestige etc.
    • Challenge – proving something, having an impact on the world, personal development
    • Independence and control – being their own boss, able to make decisions, exert power, freedom)
  • Briefly ask whether any students were able to provide an example – look to take one in each category.
  • Tell students: Those three types of non-financial objective all relate to the individuals within a company (often the owner(s)) but another type of non-financial objective is a ‘social objective’ – these are objectives which benefit a wider range of people or issues than just those relating to the owner or individuals within the business.
  • Tell students: All types of non-financial objectives are relevant to the owners of larger businesses. For example, growing smaller businesses into large ones can be a real source of challenge and lead to personal satisfaction.
  • Explain that the type of business and its owner will influence its non-financial objectives and how much influence these have on the business’s overall aims.
  • Tell students: In this subject, you will also hear ‘businesses’ referred to as ‘firms’. This is just another word for a business and you can use either.
  • Tell students: Big firms which are owned by a large number of different people may focus more on financial objectives such as profits. On the other hand, many smaller companies are set up and grown by people who want to achieve social objectives: that means they want to improve human well-being or safeguard the natural environment. It is still common in large firms for there to be a focus on both non-financial and financial objectives. For example, Apple has successfully achieved many financial objectives but has also focused on achieving the UN’s Global Climate Action award for its use of 100% green energy.
  • Explain that firms can belong either to the private or the public sector. Private sector firms are run by individuals for their own motives, whether financial or non-financial. Public sector firms are run by the government on behalf of the people of the country.
  • Show Question 1 and ask students to discuss the categorisations with their partner for 2 minutes. Choose a few pairs to justify their choices and look for understanding of the public and private sectors as explained above.

  • Ask students: Can anyone explain what type of objectives the school or hospital is likely to have? (Possible answers: Providing education/helping sick people).
  • Summarise and suggest that many of these objectives are likely to be seen as social objectives because they focus on improving some aspect of other people’s lives. Some students may give financial objectives. These are not incorrect, but social objectives may be of more importance to the public sector.
  • Explain that in the public sector, firms generally aim to provide a service to the people who live in that society: for example, almost all countries have a police force run by the government with the objective of keeping the public safe from crime.
  • Explain that some firms which are not public sector organisations also focus on social objectives. Examples include charities such as Water.org, which was set up with an objective of ensuring access to safe water and sanitation in 13 of the poorest countries in the world.
  • Introduce the idea of corporate social responsibility by telling students: More and more firms are focusing on non-financial objectives such as worker welfare, environmental impact or charitable donations. This could be because these activities create a positive image of the firm in the public’s view and may lead to increased sales or profit, or just out of a genuine sense of commitment to being good corporate citizens.

Task 1: Matching activity (3 minutes)

  • Show Slideshow: Objectives and categories and ask students to sort the objectives into four categories.
  • After three minutes quickly show the suggested answers on Slide 2 and allow any questions. Explain that the categorisation is not absolute (‘be the best in my chosen field’ may lead to personal satisfaction but might generally be seen as providing a strong challenge to the individual).
  • The important thing is that students are given some examples within categories and remember the four headings.

Task 2: Personal objectives questionnaire activity (11 minutes)

  • Give students Worksheet 2 (personal objectives questionnaire) which they should complete individually.
  • Students should find the average mark for each section and see if the marks reflect reality in terms of which section is most important to them at work. You may like to discuss if anyone was surprised by their results.

Support

For students who struggle with non-financial aims and objectives:

  • In Teaching session 1, some students may find Question 1 a bit abstract. You could encourage them to consider why Elon Musk might have started Tesla or SpaceX when he had already made $175 m from selling PayPal (or use another well-known entrepreneur as a prompt).
  • In Task 1 of Student work 1, students may have difficulty categorising a few of the objectives. Explain that this is not surprising as some of these are subjective: provided they have a clear justification for their decision, this is absolutely fine.
  • In Task 2 of Student work 1, students may not remember how to calculate an average (mean) value. Remind them that they need to add up their scores for each and then divide that total by that number of questions in the section.
  • In Task 2 of Student work 1, some students may find it difficult to answer questions that talk about careers/employment/working life. Suggest that they base their answers on their feelings about their school work.

Answering 2- and 3-mark exam questions

  • Tell students: Last lesson we looked at answering 1-mark exam questions. Today we will be focusing on 2- and 3-mark exam questions.
  • Ask students: What can you remember from last week? How many marks would you expect to get for a ‘define’ question? (Answer: 1 mark) What type of exam skill are ‘define’ questions testing – knowledge, application or analysis? (Answer: Knowledge)
  • Tell students: Questions that use the command word ‘explain’ will also require you to demonstrate your knowledge, but in a more developed form. First, you need to provide a basic answer to the question, which will usually be in the form of an impact, cause, method, advantage etc. (Such as ‘explain one disadvantage’ or ‘explain one reason that’) This will earn you 1 mark.
  • Tell students: You can earn the remaining 2 marks by providing two linked points to explain why that answer is relevant.
  • Display Slide 1 of Slideshow: Answering 2- and 3-mark exam questions. Give students 1 minute to think about a basic answer to the question.
  • Briefly show some possible ‘reasons’ on Slide 2. Tell students: three elements are needed for full marks and each should build on the previous element.
  • Click on Slide 3 to reveal a proposed answer to the question on the slide. Ask students to: Identify where (if at all) marks could be awarded for the response. Remember that only one reason can attract a mark and further marks must be for development or explanation of that stated reason. Students should work individually and spend 1 minute on this. (Any student finishing early could suggest how to improve the answer.)
  • Ask a student how many marks they have awarded and to specify where.
  • Show Slide 4 to reveal how an examiner might mark the response.
  • Click on Slide 5 to show another question. Tell students: Working individually, spend 1 minute thinking of a ‘basic answer’ for the first mark. You can come up with some alternative ‘basic answers’ once you’ve chosen an initial one. Briefly take suggestions of answers. (Possible answers: Motivation or self-fulfilment) Answers could be written on the board for students to use with their next task.
  • Click on Slide 6 to reveal a number of phrases on the slide: ‘This means that’, ‘determined to give everything’, ‘work as hard as possible’, ‘therefore’, ‘strive to do their best’, ‘ best possible service to customers.’
  • Give students 2 minutes, working independently, to write down an answer to the question in their exercise books, using some of the phrases on the slide.
  • Tell students that a first step might be to explain why motivation will improve and then the final element could be why this improved motivation is useful to the business.
  • Ask for a student to give a full-length answer to the question and ask the rest of the class to listen closely to identify whether there are three distinct ‘elements’ to the question which could each be awarded a mark. (In the manner they critiqued the answer to the previous question (Slides 3 and 4)). (Possible answers might include ‘…motivated (1 mark) to do a better job because they find the work satisfying. This means that they are more likely to work hard (1 mark) and so provide the best possible level of service to their customers (1 mark).)
  • Tell students: Another type of command word is ‘outline’. This requires you to identify an advantage, reason, method etc. (as with ‘explain’) but ‘outline’ signals that you need to give one linked development that is in the context of the business. This means that you need to relate your advantage/method etc. to something in the information you are given about the business.
  • Show an example on Slide 7 of a question. Take suggestions of a possible answer if time. Click to reveal the response. and explain where the ‘disadvantage’, which would score a knowledge mark, is and where the ‘linked development’, which would be seen as application, could come from.
  • Tell students: The other type of 2- or 3-mark question uses the command word ‘calculate’.
  • Click on Slide 8 and show an example of a ‘calculate’ question. Tell students: This question type is more straightforward: you simply need to calculate a numerical answer. Remember to follow any instructions the question gives about how to present your answer – you might be asked to give your answer to 2 decimal places, or as a currency value such as £ for (UK pounds sterling or Tks for the Bangladesh Taka).
  • Give students 2 minutes to complete the calculation on Slide 8. They should work individually, in their exercise books.
  • Show Slide 9 and take them through the answer and how marks are awarded.

Task 1: Completing 2- and 3-mark exam questions

  • Hand out Worksheet 3 to students.
  • Explain that they have 9 minutes complete the questions individually (1 minute per mark).
  • Tell them that Question 1 and Question 2 require them to use the information provided to earn the second of the 2 marks, but that Question 4 does not have any supporting information about a company and so does not need any reference to a specific business.
  • When they have finished, share findings.
  • Ask a student for their ‘reason’ in response to question 1 and then ask for the associated link to Starbucks. Work through Question 2 in the same way.
  • Take students through the calculation for Question 3.
    Display Slideshow: Suggested answer to Question 4 and ask students to decide where any marks should be awarded and why.
  • Ask a volunteer for their view on how to ‘mark’ the suggested answer.

Support

For students who struggle with 2-mark and 3-mark exam questions:

  • In Teaching session 2, if students struggle with ‘explain’ questions, suggest that they first focus on identifying a simple, one- or two-word answer which represents an advantage, reason or method that relates to the question. This will secure 1 mark and can be a springboard to unlocking the remaining marks. Show Slide 4 of the slideshow to illustrate an example of a short initial response. Students may want to relate the question to a business they are really familiar with and could even use that example to extend their simple answer.
  • In Slideshow: Answering 2- and 3-mark exam questions (Slide 8), students who struggle with the calculation for profit (Sales revenue – Total costs) might initially ensure that they understand the difference between a cost and a revenue, then try and identify which of the four items in the table fit which category before finally applying the formula (which can be given to them if they cannot remember it from Lesson 1).
  • In Worksheet 3, question 3, if students struggle with the calculation (calculating market shares), explain that ‘market leader’ simply means the firm with the largest market share so will be the company that sells more boats (or in other instances had sold the greatest value of goods). Then direct students to p. 286 of the Student Book, where a formula is given for calculating market share, and explain that the market leader’s sales volume would be the numerator and the sum of all sales in the market would be the denominator.
  • Hand out Worksheet 4.
  • Ask students to work individually to produce a poster which sets out certain information about the type of job which they would like to do in the future.
  • Show the class the following multiple-choice questions on Slideshow: Financial and non-financial objectives and ask them to write their answers on a mini whiteboard or piece of paper.
  • After showing each question, ask students to show whiteboards when ready and discuss any misconceptions. Ask for volunteers to explain why their chosen answer is correct.
  • Students should complete Lesson 2: Homework sheet.
  • Remind them that they should refer to the case study ‘Doha Airline Meals’ (found in the Student Book, p. 15) in ‘outline’ questions, but that ‘explain’ questions do not need any reference to a specific business.
  • This activity covers:
    • Q1 - This relates to one specific type of non-financial objective and requires students to undertake a piece of extended writing
    • Q2 - A recap of financial objectives from L1 (LO1) which is also revisited during the starter to make the distinction between financial and non-financial objectives but requiring a response based on the exam-technique elements covered in this lesson’s Teaching session 2
    • Q3 and Q4 - These questions require students to use the content they have covered in Teaching session 1 regarding different types of non-financial objective but to provide answers in the exam-style format which they have learned about in Teaching session 2.

Alternative activities

  • These resources can be used if you are splitting the content of this lesson into two sessions.

Non-financial reasons for starting your own business

  • Tell students: Imagine you had all the money you could ever need in the world. What non-financial reasons might you have for starting your own business?
  • Ask students to discuss their reasons in pairs for 1 minute. Then ask volunteers to share reasons with the class. (Possible answers: Care/employment for others; positive environmental impacts; developing great ideas; challenging themselves)
  • Ask students: Can you suggest underlying purposes for one or more of the objectives? (Possible answers: Raising global living standards, environmental stewardship, personal fulfilment)
  • Tell students: These reasons will be at the centre of what we learn today. Developing them will be important for extending your answers to examination questions.

Discussing the questionnaire

  • In this activity students will discuss the results of Student work 1, Task 2.
  • Ask students: Did the outcome of the questionnaire surprise you? Did you learn anything about what type of non-financial objectives motivate you? Do you feel you would be more motivated by financial or non-financial objectives? Why?
  • Ask pairs to spend 2 minutes discussing their ideas and then ask volunteers to describe their responses to the group.
