Learning objectives

Learning objectives

In this lesson, students will learn to:

  • understand what globalisation is
  • understand the factors that lead to globalisation.

Key definitions

Subject vocabulary

  • emerging economies: rapidly growing economies such as Brazil or India
  • globalisation: the growing integration of the world’s economies, businesses and people
  • intellectual property (IP): people’s knowledge or creative ideas that have a commercial value and are protectable under different laws
  • interdependence: when the actions of one business will have an effect on another business
  • saturate (market): to offer so much of a product for sale that there is more than people want to buy
  • specialisation: focusing on producing one (or a few) goods to benefit from increased efficiency as a result

 

General vocabulary

  • containerisation: a shipping method where large amounts of materials are packed into large, identical containers to make transport more efficient

Teaching session 1

Features of globalisation

Features of globalisation

  • Globalisation is the growing integration of the world’s economies.
  • It allows businesses to produce goods wherever they want in the world.
  • Materials, workers, technology are used in whichever country has the most appropriate supplies.
  • Businesses can then sell their products across the world.

Reasons for globalisation

Image

ACDG

Building a chain of reasoning

Expand

Container ships can transport far greater quantities of goods than was previously possible.

Expand

This has reduced the cost of selling goods in foreign markets, allowing businesses to offer lower prices while still making more profits.

Expand

These profits encourage more businesses to think globally and design products that appeal to consumers regardless of nationality, thus increasing globalisation.

Image

How does increased tourism lead to greater globalisation?

Expand

Increased levels of tourism mean that people are far more used to travelling internationally and meeting people from different countries, with different cultures and traditions.

Expand

This exposes them to new products meaning they are more likely to try such goods, allowing businesses to access a wider range of consumers.

Expand

This increases the attractiveness to businesses of selling their goods in foreign markets and so increases the speed of globalisation.

Factors leading to globalisation

  • Improved transport networks
  • Greater tourism
  • Developments in technology (advent of computers, smartphones and the internet)
  • Businesses wishing to sell abroad
  • Government actions

  1. Write an answer to the question below.

c Finish the answer by writing a final sentence.

Question:

Explain one factor that has led to increased globalisation over the last 20 years (3 marks)

Example answer:

Government actions such as privatisating companies that were previously state-owned has created more opportunities for businesses to be privately run with profit as a key objective. This has meant that foreign businesses who provide a service in one country may be able to run newly privatised businesses in another country. This is because they have developed expertise and can use this in the new country, bringing new ideas across international boundaries and contributing to globalisation.
