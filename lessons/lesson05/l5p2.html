Legal structures of sole traders and partnerships

  • Tell students: Different businesses are set up to have different legal structures. A legal structure refers to how a company is owned and who runs it, as well as how the government treats it. Today’s lesson will focus on two distinct forms of legal structure that share some common features: sole traders and partnerships. Any legal structures that govern how a company functions can also be referred to as ‘forms of ownership’.
  • Tell students: Both sole traders and partnerships are unincorporated businesses. This means the law sees no separation or distinction between the owner (or owners) and the company.
  • Tell students: This lack of separation between the person and the business means that if the business owes money that it cannot repay, the owner or owners are personally responsible for its debts.
  • This means that the owner is liable to repay all of the money that their company owes, and so may be forced to sell their own assets, such as a car or house, to repay the business’s debts. However, this would generally only happen if the business becomes bankrupt. Therefore, this type of company is referred to as having unlimited liability.
  • If students are unsure about the meanings of ‘liable’ and ‘assets’, remind them that they mean ‘responsible’ and ‘possessions’.
  • Tell students: In contrast, companies with limited liability are known as incorporated businesses. The owners have a separate legal identity from their business meaning they  are not liable for all of the debts of those companies: they can only lose the value of the money they have personally invested into the company rather than their personal possessions.    
  • Explain to students that businesses with unlimited liability, such as sole traders and partnerships, tend to be much smaller than limited liability (incorporated) businesses and much more simply structured and managed.

Support

  • In Teaching session 1 some students may struggle with the concept that a sole trader may be the only business owner but that the business can still employ other people. Ask students to consider what would happen if a sole trader had to work alone: never being able to have holidays, afford to be absent through illness etc. Stress that it is just the ownership of a sole trader that rests with just one individual, and many such businesses will have other employees.
  • In Student work 1, in Question 1 (a) on Worksheet 1, some students may not understand what a ‘separate legal identity’ means. Explain that companies are only seen to have been formally created (incorporated) when they are registered as a limited company with the government and tax authorities. Therefore, sole traders and partnerships benefit from much quicker and easier set-up than limited (incorporated companies). Therefore, a separate legal identity is when the incorporated business is treated (under law) as a different ‘entity’ or ‘thing’ to the owner(s).

 

Comparing sole traders and partnerships

  • Tell students: A partnership is a business which shares many of the features of a sole trader, such as unlimited liability and being unincorporated, more than one person owns it.
  • Ask students to remember the disadvantages of being a sole trader that they learned earlier in the lesson (Teaching session 1 and Worksheet 1, Question 4).
  • Ask them: Do these disadvantages apply to just sole traders, or do some of them also affect partnerships? If so, which ones?
  • Give students 1 minute to consider their answers. They should work individually.
  • Take feedback. Ask students: Can you name the two disadvantages that would only apply to a sole trader? (Answers: The owner has to make all the decisions which might be stressful or difficult; the owner will often have to work long hours.)
  • Ask students: How could a sole trader get help with important decisions and managing their workload?
  • Share ideas briefly as a class before explaining that some sole traders might seek one or more partners to help them run the business.
  • Tell students: A partnership is a business with unlimited liability that is owned by between 2 and 20 people who work together to manage the business, making decisions and sharing the profits (or losses) between themselves.
  • Show Slide 1 of the Slideshow: Benefits of partnerships.  
  • Go through the information on the slide with students, then tell them to copy the information into their exercise books.
  • Explain to students that the key feature of unincorporated companies, unlimited liability, is still the case for partnerships but that having additional owners can have several benefits:
    • Having extra support for managing the business and making decisions
    • Having more owners allows for tasks to be divided up so they can specialise in what they are best at
    • With more owners, there is more scope for raising money. This can be used to help the business survive when first launched or to grow at a later stage.
  • Tell students: Many companies that provide professional services, where workers need specific qualifications and may have studied for many years, are partnerships. Dentists, lawyers and architects often operate in partnerships.
  • Remind students that it is possible to move from being a sole trader to a partnership (or any other form of company) at any stage of the business’s life – it does not have to be decided when first starting up.
  • Click on Slide 2.
  • Give students two minutes to identify which disadvantages of being a partnership do not also relate to sole traders. They should work individually, noting the correct answers in their exercise books.
  • Take feedback. Ask volunteers to suggest answers and discuss whether each is correct or not.
  • Click to reveal the answers.

Ask students: Look at the options again. What are the advantages of being in a partnership rather than being a sole trader? (Answer: i)

  • Ask students: What advantages are common to a partnership and a sole trader? (Answers: d, e)
  • Tell students: Although profits have to be shared in a partnership, it would be expected that overall profits would likely be higher because there are more people involved in the business.
  • Tell students: As both partnerships and sole traders are unincorporated businesses where there is no distinction between the owner and the business itself, if an owner in a partnership dies, that ‘entity’ or business will also cease to exist – because an owner has.
  • Tell students: This means that the remaining options of there not being anyone to help with difficult decisions and it being more difficult to raise finance are both disadvantages of being a sole trader compared to a partnership.
  • Explain that having to do what the majority of owners want can be a significant disadvantage if one owner disagrees strongly, especially because the unlimited liability can mean that owner is personally liable for losses arising from a decision they did not agree with or support. This is often a source of disagreement within partnerships and can lead to the partnership being dissolved.
  • Tell students: One way of avoiding issues created by disagreements is to have a deed of partnership. This document is not mandatory but helps by setting out details of how the partners agree to work together and resolve any issues.

Support

For students who struggle with Comparing sole traders and partnerships:

  • In Teaching session 2, students may struggle with the task on Slide 2 because they do not understand what they are supposed to select as disadvantages. Suggest there are three categories, and they need to choose only those that fit into the third category:
    • Advantages of being a partnership/sole trader
    • Disadvantages common to both partnerships and sole traders
    • Disadvantages of being a partnership as opposed to being a sole trader.

They may benefit from thinking about problems that may occur if they were to change from being a sole trader to taking on a new partner and becoming a partnership.
