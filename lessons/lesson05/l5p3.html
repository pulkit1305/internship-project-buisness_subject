Learning objectives

Learning objectives

In this lesson, students will learn to understand the distinction between unincorporated and incorporated businesses.

Key definitions

Subject vocabulary

  • deed of partnership: a legally binding document that states the formal rights of partners in a business
  • incorporated business: a business that has a separate legal identity from its owners
  • limited liability: when the business owner is only liable for the original amount of money invested in the business
  • partnership: a business owned by between 2 and 20 people
  • percentage change: change divided by the original value then multiplied by 100
  • sole trader: a business owned by a single person
  • unincorporated business:a business where there is no legal separation between the owner and the business
  • unlimited liability:when the owner of a business is personally liable for the debts of the business.    

Key definition

General vocabulary

  • legal structure: the way the law views how a business is set up, owned and run

Features of a sole trader

  • Only one owner (but can employ other people)
  • Unlimited liability (responsible for the repayment of all a business’s debt)
  • Unincorporated so no legal distinction between business and owner
  • Usually a small business.

Advantages of being a sole trader

  • It is quick, easy and inexpensive to set up as a sole trader
  • The ongoing administration costs are low
  • The owner keeps all of the profit made
  • The owner is in complete control of the business
  • Financial information does not have to be published.

Disadvantages of being a sole trader

  • Sole traders have unlimited liability
  • It can be more difficult to raise finance
  • The owner has to make all the decisions, which might be stressful
  • The owner will often have to work long hours
  • Lack of continuity: when the owner dies, the business ceases.

Benefits of partnerships

A partnership is a business with unlimited liability that is owned by between 2 and 20 people who work together to manage a business, making decisions and sharing the profits (or losses between) themselves

Benefits of partnership compared to sole trader status

  • partners can share the workload
  • partners share decision-making
  • partners can specialise
  • partners can all contribute money to make it easier to expand the business.

Question 3

Setting up a business – key points to cover

 

  • Name and overview of what the business does.
  • Which form of legal structure (ownership) the business will have.
  • Three key objectives of the business/its owners.
  • Three reasons why you have chosen that form of ownership (which relate specifically to you or the business itself).
  • Three specific disadvantages of your chosen form of ownership (again explaining why each is relevant to your particular business) and how you plan to overcome them.

     An example answer (sole trader):

  • My business is called Top Tips and will be a nail
  • The business will be a sole trader, owned and run by me.
  • The main objectives are to survive the first six  months after opening and then make a profit of at least £10,000 in the remainder of the year. The business will also have the objective of having at least 250 different customers registered onto the database by the end of the first year.
  • Top Tips will be a sole trader because:
    • I will be in business on my own and am confident running a business as I have previously operated a hair salon (so I do not need any partners.)
    • My business will not run up many debts (paying the majority of bills upfront). So the risk of having unlimited liability should not be too high.
    • I will be able to keep all of the business's profits and do not need specialist advice from anyone else. This also gives me the flexibility to hire additional staff when customers have sufficient interest  to merit having more staff.
    • I do not want to involve another owner in decision-making because I think it could lead to disagreements and mean that it takes too long to make decisions.
  • I understand that the following disadvantages of being a sole trader could affect Top Tips:
    • It may be difficult for me to find enough cash for a deposit on a salon and to buy the equipment I need because I will not have any other partners who are willing to put money into the business. (I plan to use my savings to resolve this issue.)
    • If the business becomes bankrupt, I will have to repay any debts the business has myself and could lose my family home. (I do not think losing my home is likely as once the business is running, I will not have many costs – just the rent, which I will pay in advance out of my personal savings.)
    • If I wanted to expand into providing another product or service (such as facial treatments), I do not have any expertise  so this might      be difficult without a partner who knows about that aspect of business.

An example answer (partnership):

  • Our business is called King & Ormsby and will be a small accountancy company.
  • The company will be a partnership with two partners, owned and run by me and a friend, who are both qualified accountants.
  • The main objectives are to grow the business so that we can both earn more personal income than we did when we were employed by large accountancy companies. The business will also have the objective of having at least £200,000 in sales after the first year.
  • King & Ormsby will be a partnership because:
    • The business will not take large amounts of risk  and so does not need to have limited liability. We are also insured against any losses.
    • We chose a partnership because we both specialise in different aspects of accounting and want to appeal to clients who may need a range of services.
    • We want to be able to keep all of the profits (shared 50/50) rather than work for a company that keeps most of the income we earn to cover its overheads and then makes a large profit based on our work. We do not feel we need so much administrative support and could run our company more efficiently.
    • Having another partner will be useful for us both to have a trusted and knowledgeable person to discuss difficult business decisions with.
  • I understand that the following disadvantages of being a partnership could affect King & Ormsby:
    • We do not have large levels of personal savings and so will have to borrow to start the company, and this could be difficult as a partnership because we do not have much collateral to offer lenders so may not be able to raise sufficient money to rent premises, carry out marketing etc.
    • If the business becomes bankrupt, we will have to repay any debts the business has, and so could become personally indebted. This could also affect our ability to borrow in the future and start other businesses and damage our reputation as good financial managers or advisers.
    • Becoming a limited company might persuade some larger clients that we are a reputable business; however, we plan to work with smaller organisations and offer them better value for money so we have chosen to run the lowest cost form of ownership to support this aim.

c             Read the information at the bottom of p. 21 about deeds of partnership.

  • Produce a partnership deed for Kisuli, Okumu and Owino’s business. Ensure the deed of partnership covers:
  • all of the points listed on p. 21 of the Student Book

any other elements that you think would be helpful to the owners in running their business without disputes.

  1. b A deed of partnership may be helpful because it sets out an agreement between partners so that they all know what has been agreed about how the partnership will function. This can be referred to in the event of disagreements so they can be resolved quickly. The deed will act as a set of ‘rules’ so that each partner knows what they can expect of the others and what is expected of them.
  2. Example answer

Deed of partnership ‒ Kisuli, Okumu and Owino

  1. Each partner will contribute KES 2m to provide finance to start the business.
  2. Profits and losses will be shared equally between the owners because they all contributed the same amount of money to start it up and will all work full-time in the business.
  3. The partnership can be dissolved if any of the three partners requests this in writing, giving three months’ notice. Should this happen, the partners will seek two independent valuations of the business’s assets, and an average valuation will be taken of those two. The partners will each share that value equally once the business has either been sold to another company or the assets sold off to raise money.
  4. Each partner will have one vote on important decisions and majority decision-making will be respected.

If  all three partners agree to admit a new partner, this will be allowed (although each owner will have a veto if they do not approve the person recommended by any other partner). The value of the business will be agreed (as in point 3 above), and the new partner will be expected to contribute capital equivalent to one-third of that value.
