Key features of public limited companies

  • Tell students: Today we will be looking at public limited companies. This name can be abbreviated to plc in the UK or Inc. in the USA. They are also known as AG in Germany and SA in France. In your exams you will always need to use plc.
  • Tell them: Public limited companies are incorporated: just like private limited companies. As we learned in Lesson 5, this means the company has a separate legal entity from the owners, who therefore have limited liability. However, unlike private limited companies, there will be many, many shareholders.
  • Tell them: Public limited companies tend to be our biggest companies and the reason is in their name. They are the only form of company which can sell shares directly to the public. The reason there are so many owners is that shares are available to buy on the stock market and, therefore, anyone who thinks the company will make money can purchase part of the ownership of it.
  • Tell them: If a company believes it can attract enough people to want to buy shares in it, it can undertake a stock market flotation: also called ‘going public’. This means that a private limited company can agree to create millions of new shares in the company which will be available for sale to the public from a certain date.
  • Explain that, after this date, any shareholder wishing to sell any of their shares in the newly created public limited company can do so easily on the stock market.
  • Tell students: This means that there is a secondary market for the shares in the company and investors buy and sell many thousands of them every minute of every day. However, the money those shareholders receive for selling their shares does not get returned to the company because the shares belonged to the shareholders and so they get to keep the returns and any financial gains on any resale.

Support

For students who struggle with Key features of public limited companies:

  • In Teaching session 1 some students will struggle to understand why the ‘re-sale’ of shares (after an initial stock market flotation) does not provide capital for the company, but instead does for the shareholders themselves. Use the analogy of having baked a cake. It takes some resources to ‘prepare’ the cake: your time, the cost of ingredients, your secret recipe, the costs of power, renting the kitchen from your parents. If you try a slice of the cake and think it is amazing and other people will also love it you could try and sell further ‘shares’ in it. You might split it into 11 slices (plus the one you have already eaten.) You decide to sell 7 ‘shares’ and keep 4 for your family. The 7 people you sell shares (slices) of the cake to now own those shares and have had to give you money for them. That money you receive pays for your initial costs (butter, time etc.) and you will hopefully have quite a lot of spare money which represents your ‘return on investment’ – the ‘extra reward’ you can expect to receive because you were willing to risk the cake being a flop and therefore suffering losses (buying butter etc.) However, after other shareholders bought a slice of the cake, they now own the rights to that slice and so can choose to sell it on themselves: you would not expect them to give you (or the company) the money they receive from doing so – it is the same with re-selling shares in a plc.

  • Lead a class discussion, using the following questions.
  • Ask a volunteer: Why may plcs have a high profile in the media? (Answer: Because once a business is a public company, its financial results, share prices etc. are available to everyone and so become part of stories that are reported by the press, because their audience may be interested in buying or selling those or other shares. This creates publicity for the company.)
  • Ask a volunteer: Why may plcs experience economies of scale? (Answer: Because plcs are large companies they will expect to have lower costs (on average) than smaller companies. One reason might be the bulk-buying discounts they can expect to receive from suppliers.)
  • Ask a volunteer: Why may plcs have some degree of market power? (Answer: Large companies that dominate their market (as plcs can do) can become so powerful that they can establish an ‘expected standard’ for how a service is delivered or for how much it is ‘normal’ to charge for a produce. Other, smaller, companies often have to follow their lead even if they do not want to.)
  • Ask a volunteer: Why may the managers of plcs be able to run the company in their own interests, rather than those of the owners? (Answer: Because plcs have thousands of shareholders, they cannot control day-to-day decision-making in the business. Therefore, they trust directors to do this for them, but those directors may take decisions with a view to protecting their own jobs or achieving financial bonuses or pay rises, rather than increasing the amount of profit the company makes or how much it is worth, which is what the owners want them to do.)
  • Tell students: it is important to remember these advantages and disadvantages as they will allow you to justify decisions you make about what the best form of ownership is for different companies.

Answers

H Public limited company because the business is already of a reasonable size (medium-sized) and so is likely to be a ltd at the moment. It wishes to grow quickly and the best way to achieve this will be through investing money into developing new products and promoting these to customers which will mean it needs to increase the number of products it can produce. All of these activities will require large amounts of money and, because the company is already quite successful, the best way to achieve this is likely to be by going public and becoming a plc.

F Private limited company because the business has been run by one owner for many years so will currently be either a sole trader or a private limited company. The owner wishes to bring her daughter into the business to share responsibilities and learn about the company so it can no longer be a sole trader. Becoming a partnership is a possibility but the ‘situation’ stated the owner wanted her daughter to be a shareholder and so this can only occur with a limited company. Private limited company is the only ‘limited’ option because the company is not big enough to go public.

G Sole trader because the two owners feel they cannot work together to manage the company any longer so must dissolve their partnership. Because they both know the industry well and want to continue within it as individuals a sole trader status is likely to suit both of them best as this form of ownership will be easy to establish and reflect their current levels of liability.

H Private limited company because the business is described as ‘large, with a powerful shareholder’ so it must be a limited company to have shareholders. This shareholder wants to gain full control of the business and so will need to buy the remaining shares from their current owners, which would allow them to change the ownership from a public to private limited company (or just to remain a limited company but with fewer owners).

M Public limited company is the only option because the business wants to allow 2800 staff to each share a small proportion of the ownership (and therefore the shares) of the company. This means they will need to float the company, creating a lot of new shares so the current owners receive many thousands and the employees can each be given a few to reflect their contributions to the company.

N Partnership as there will be two owners a sole trader would not be appropriate. The simplest forms of business to operate are unincorporated companies as they do not require any specific paperwork to be completed so a partnership would be best.
