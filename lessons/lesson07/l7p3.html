Learning objectives

Learning objectives

In this lesson, students will learn to:

  • understand the main business characteristics of public limited companies linked to size
  • examine the most appropriate form of ownership for different types of business.

Key definitions

Subject vocabulary

  • public limited company: a limited company in which shares can be sold to the general public and traded on a stock exchange
  • stock market flotation: when a private limited company first makes its shares available for the public to buy on the stock exchange (this means it becomes a public limited company)

 

General vocabulary

  • going public: the process of changing from a private limited company to a public limited company. This is sometimes called ‘flotation’

Key features of PLCs

Pair: Discuss your answers with a partner.

Features of a public limited company (plc)

  • Shares bought and sold on the stock market
  • Able to raise large amounts of money when they go public
  • Shareholders can re-sell shares to anyone else
  • Company has to publish detailed reports about financial performance
  • Reports must be audited (checked) by a qualified, independent accountant
  • Shareholders give day-to-day responsibility for running the company to a board of directors

Advantages and disadvantages

Worksheet 1, Question 2

‘Continuity on owner’s death’ (1b) – a feature of both private limited companies and public limited companies.

  • There is a separation between the shareholder and the company. Any owner who dies can leave their shares to a friend or family member who then takes over that part of the ownership.
  • The company can then carry on unaffected, because the same number of shares is still owned – just by different people.

‘Ownership of shares can be controlled’ (1e) – a feature of a private limited company

  • The shares of plcs are traded openly (to the public) on a stock exchange.
  • Any owner can sell their shares without having to ask permission from anyone else.
  • This will allow new owners to gain ownership regardless of other owners’ wishes – provided they pay to buy at least one share.

‘Potentially vulnerable to takeovers’ (1f) – a feature of a public limited company

  • If original founders decide to keep some shares themselves when they ‘float’ the company, they are still very unlikely to own a majority of the shares.
  • They cannot control what happens to the remaining shares.
  • If investors or companies buy enough shares (which all have an equal voting ‘right’) then decisions can be controlled by them rather than the original founders and shareholders.

Advantages of being a public limited company

  1. Limited liability for the owners
  2. May have high profile in the media
  3. Companies may enjoy some degree of market power (e.g. price setting power)
  4. Can raise very large amounts of share capital
  5. Companies may experience economies of scale

Disadvantages of being a public limited company

  1. Have to share profits with many thousands of other shareholders
  2. Expensive to set up this form of company ownership
  3. Managers may run the company in their own interests rather than shareholders’ interests
  4. High on-going administration costs
  5. Vulnerable to takeovers
  6. Lots of financial information available to competitors/customers
  7. Shareholders cannot control who else buys shares and therefore some control of the company
