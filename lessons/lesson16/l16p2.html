The opportunities of globalisation for businesses

  • Tell students:
    • we have seen how globalisation can bring about general opportunities to people around the world. Now we are going to focus more specifically on the opportunities that arise for businesses.
    • In 1972 a small company in Oregon, USA released its first ever sneaker or training shoe: the Moon Shoe. Fifty years later Nike earned revenues of nearly $45 billion and profits of $6 billion. This growth could not have occurred without globalisation.
    • One of the key factors that allowed Nike to grow so quickly, from being a provider of track shoes to American college athletes to a worldwide sports brand, has been the way that globalisation has given the company access to much larger markets.
    • China is now the company’s second biggest market and is crucial to its future growth prospects. This is because average incomes in the country are rising rapidly and Chinese consumers are more exposed to global brands such as Nike through social media and worldwide sports events. This includes the American NBA Basketball on TV, and so consumers are exposed to Nike’s high levels of sponsorship of the sport.

  • Explain to students: The bold text represents the opportunities of operating globally for Nike, while the underlined text shows features that drive globalisation. The remaining statements are generally seen as threats to selling products in different countries.
  • Ask a student for a suggestion about how one of the opportunities arises.
  • Possible answers:
    • Selling the same product in many markets means far more products are supplied, therefore companies can use specialist production machinery which reduces the average cost of making each product. This is an example of an economy of scale when producing goods.
    • Offering the same product to consumers in many places allows companies to receive revenue from a range of countries, so if one market experiences an increase in competition or a recession, the others can hopefully cover for any reduction in sales.
  • Tell students: Different opportunities are often interlinked. For example, whenconsidering the impact of an increased market size on costs, we saw on the slide that a larger market may allow a business to benefit from economies of scale.
  • If preparing an explanation, you could simply state a ‘reduction in costs’ as a separate opportunity of globalisation for large companies or you could continue with the earlier analysis to link to those lower costs and develop your argument further. For example, you could explain that lower costs allow a business to be more competitive against other rivals that do not experience those cost advantages.
  • If a company like Nike benefits from economies of scale, they could spend the additional revenue on sponsoring star athletes who are recognised across the world and so associate themselves with the sporting success of those athletes and increase sales of sports clothing.
  • Alternatively, large companies such as Nike could use their lower costs to create a competitive advantage by sponsoring global events like the World Cup which only the largest companies can do because it is so expensive. However, the benefit of doing so is that virtually every country in the world televises the competition and so its ‘reach’ is massive.
  • Ask students: Can you think of any other opportunities to businesses of globalisation?
  • Possible answers:
    • New product ideas may come from anywhere in the world, offering opportunities to obtain a wider range of possible new features or ways of producing a good.
    • If consumer tastes become more universal as a result of globalisation and increased communication between countries, it will be simpler to sell the same products globally without having to vary designs or advertising campaigns.
  • Click on Slide 3 and tell students to make a note of the information displayed.
  • Ask students: Why might businesses find that globalisation gives them better opportunities to access labour?
  • Give students 1 minute to discuss their ideas with a partner. If necessary, explain that by labour you mean the workers that companies need to produce and sell their goods.
  • Ask for volunteers to share their ideas. Explain the following points to students if they do not suggest them.
  • Possible answers:
    • Companies that need workers with specific skills (e.g. tech companies seeking software engineers) will find improved freedom of movement allows migration and so companies can attract those highly talented workers.
    • Where people move to seek work, it means companies have a wider choice of workers to choose from and so this increased supply means that wages can remain lower, reducing costs.
    • Footloose companies can relocate their production to where labour costs (the average amount companies have to pay a worker to produce a good) are much lower.
  • Tell students: Another way that multinational companies benefit from globalisation is the opportunity to pay less tax. Large companies increasingly choose to base themselves in the country which offers the lowest rate of tax on profits made there. For example, Apple has large parts of its European business in Ireland where the rate of corporation tax is 12.5%. If those activities were taking place in France or Germany, they would have to pay 28.4% or 29.9% of profits in tax.
  • This shows how globalisation, and the opportunities it offers to businesses to choose where they base themselves, can have a large impact on how much money the owners of the business can receive.

The threats of globalisation for businesses

  • Tell students:
    • Although there are many opportunities for companies from globalisation, there are also drawbacks.
    • Globalisation has allowed multinational companies to grow far larger, often using economies of scale to reduce prices. This has meant that some small companies have lost customers who have been attracted by lower prices.
    • It is also more difficult for those small companies to have elaborate new product launches that larger companies can afford. This means that those small companies find it more and more difficult to survive due to the greater competition they face from large multinationals.
    • Globalisation also makes it hard for small companies to compete because large, global companies can more easily enter markets due to free trade and improved transportation. They can also afford to invest in global marketing campaigns which appeal to consumers who are used to seeing advertising from global brands on social media. This has meant that many small companies can no longer compete in their home countries.
    • Specialisation is a big part of globalisation. It has exposed whole industries to increased international competition, such as manufacturing companies. Many manufacturing companies in Europe have shut down as they cannot compete with the low prices found in companies based in South Asian countries.

  • Explain that ‘international takeovers’ is another potential threat. Tell students: globalisation has made it easier for money to flow between countries and one effect of this is that it allows large multinationals to buy smaller companies in other countries. Often the management of the smaller companies may not welcome this and this situation is referred to as a hostile takeover.

  • Ask a pair to share an impact on one stakeholder Ask another pair to share an impact on a different stakeholder group. If necessary, remind students that a stakeholder is any party affected by actions taken by a business.

  • Click on Slide 3 to show ‘increased risk of external shocks’ and allow students to make a note.
  • Tell students: An external shock is an event that businesses cannot influence or control, they tend to affect all businesses within an industry or an entire country. The growing interdependence of businesses and countries that is a feature of globalisation means that countries are more exposed to external shocks which happen in one country and can spread more easily to others because of the increased inter-connectedness of the modern world.
  • Give students 1 minute to think of as many examples of external shocks as they can. These could be real events, or could be more general events that could potentially happen. They should work individually.
  • Ask students to share their examples as a class. List the ideas on the board.
  • Possible answers:
    • The COVID-19 pandemic when lockdowns forced people to stay at home and not visit shops or businesses
    • Global financial crisis of 2008 when banks reduced their lending to businesses and consumers
    • The Suez Canal 2021 blockage by the Ever Given container ship
    • Natural disasters, such as the 2011 earthquake off the coast of Japan.
  • Tell students: In 2008 a global financial crisis was triggered by events in the USA where a fall in the value of houses in America eventually led to a global recession because the financial systems of countries were so interdependent that the impacts spread around the world.
  • Taking one or two other examples from external shocks listed, invite students to try to explain how the initial event may have had much wider consequences for businesses in other countries.
  • Possible answers:
    • Natural disasters such as earthquakes (the 2011 earthquake off the coast of Japan suspended one business’s (Renesas) production of computer chips so that the business lost $156 in 3 months). However, the effects were far wider because the chips were used by car companies such as Toyota and so temporarily closed 17 of their 20 biggest factories around the world. It was estimated that car production in Japan fell by about 25% in 2011 and about 15% of all production of Japanese cars outside the country was lost.
    • Trade disruptions: when a large ship got stuck in the Suez Canal in 2021 it blocked a route which was used for 12% of the entire world’s trade. It was estimated that the closure was holding up $6.7 million worth of global trade every minute. Given that the ship was stuck for almost a week, many businesses across the world were badly affected.
  • If no students have been able to explain how an external shock might affect other businesses, provide the examples given in the possible answers above.

The O and T in SWOT stand for Opportunities and Threats and these are seen as being factors in the business’s external environment that will affect it – in other words, these are factors which are outside the business’s control.
