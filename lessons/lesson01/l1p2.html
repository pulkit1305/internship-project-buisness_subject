Lesson 1 | Teacher guidance Financial Aims and Objectives/Exam Skills 1

  • Worksheets will need to be printed for students in advance of the lesson.
  • While all care is taken to ensure resource content is in line with cultural sensitivities, please be aware that it should be reviewed before sharing with students.

Introducing the objectives of entrepreneurs

  • Ask students: What is an entrepreneur? (Answer: A person who takes risks and sets up a business.)
  • If some students are unfamiliar with the term, show the Key definition and ask students to copy the definition into their exercise books.
  • Ask students: Can you suggest reasons why people may set up their own business?
  • Take individual responses from students one at a time and list students’ ideas on the board. (Possible answers: to make money, to become famous, to bring out new products, to share ideas globally)
  • Discuss some of the reasons listed on the board, taking one reason at a time.
  • Ask students: Can you name any examples of companies/entrepreneurs that might have started for this reason?
    (Possible answers:
    • to make money: Jan Koum started WhatsApp because he dreamed of becoming rich
    • to become famous: Oprah Winfrey has described how, at the age of four, she always knew she was born for fame and a life of remarkable achievements
    • to bring out new products: Elon Musk started SpaceX to make affordable space travel a reality; Jack Ma started Alibaba because he was so excited about the opportunities the internet offered
    • to share ideas globally: Shiza Shahid worked with Malala Yousafzai to start a company to promote education for every girl)
  • Summarise this activity and explain: These reasons for starting a business can be thought of as the objectives of the founders of new businesses.
  • Tell students: The objectives of a business are the goals or targets it sets.
  • Tell students: The word ‘aims’ is used interchangeably with ‘objectives’ in business.
  • Explain: Existing businesses need to have clear objectives so that they know what they are trying to achieve and can measure how successful they are in achieving their goals or aims.

Financial objectives

  • Tell students: This lesson will focus on ‘financial objectives’.
  • Explain: Financial means ‘relating to money’ and so, generally, objectives used to measure financial success or failure will often involve units of currency such as dollars ($) or pounds (£).
  • Display Slide 1 of Slideshow: Financial objectives of businesses.
  • Give students 1 minute to review the list of different objectives that companies could have and to try to categorise them into ‘financial objectives’ and ‘non-financial objectives’. They should work individually, listing their ideas in their exercise books.
  • Go through the objectives one by one and via a show of hands, ask the class whether each objective is financial or non-financial.
  • (Answers:
    • Financial – making profit, being financially secure, selling more goods than any other company, surviving the first three months
    • non-financial – personal satisfaction, challenging yourself, being your own boss)
  • Explain: The four financial objectives all relate to money, can be measured in terms of money or will directly cost/generate money for a business.
  • Click on Slide 2.
  • Tell students: A business is likely to have five main financial objectives. Copy the terms from the board into your exercise books.
  • Provide an overview of each type of financial objective, focusing, for example, on meaning – which type of company a specific objective may be most appropriate for and why that objective may be relevant. Ask students to make notes:
    • Survival – explain: This is about earning enough money to be able to cover all of the upcoming costs that need to be paid. This financial objective applies to almost all new businesses. Some businesses will also focus on survival when there are economic issues in a country and so customers have less money to spend. All businesses have to achieve this objective even if they do not explicitly set it as an objective.
    • Profit – explain: Most business owners aim to have money left over after they have paid all of their costs and this can be thought of as ‘profit’. Profits can be spent in many different ways including providing a living for the owner(s). The amount of profit targeted will depend on many factors including size, what the company does and the general state of the economy. However, setting a numerical target is important for measuring the success of a business.
    • Sales – first explain: Before we talk about sales, it is important to know that ‘revenue’ is the money from the sale of goods and services.
      Now explain: Focusing on how many products a company can sell in a specific period of time or how much revenue a business earns can be more important than how much profit is being made. This is because businesses often want to grow and so ‘sales’ is often seen as an objective to show whether growth has been achieved, or how much growth has been achieved. Sales can be measured either through how many products are sold (which is known as sales volume) or by the total amount of money the company earned from selling its products (which is known as sales value or total revenue).
    • Market share – explain: Setting a target number for how many ‘sales’ a business will make is important but for larger companies, they often want to be seen as number one in their market. So, measuring their sales as a proportion of the total market sales – the market share – allows them to calculate this. Companies that aren’t market leaders may also want to measure market share to see how they are doing relative to their competitors.
    • Financial security – explain: Companies need to achieve financial security to provide a ‘buffer’ or cushion so that they could survive future periods using any spare money from the current period. Owners also want to be financially secure so that they have sufficient money (after paying their basic costs of living such as housing costs or food bills) to maintain their lifestyle and afford luxuries such as a car or meals out.
  • Click on Slide 3.
  • Tell students: Profit is a crucial concept to understand. It can be calculated as the difference between a company’s total revenue, which is the full value of the goods that it sells in a certain period of time, and its total cost, which is all the money spent in order to keep a company running. Tell students to make a note of the formulae for total revenue and profit.
  • Illustrate that total revenue can be calculated by SP × Q using the following example of a cake sale in school. You could demonstrate the calculation on the board for students as you work through the example together.
  • Ask students: If you bake 20 cookies and sell them for 50p each, how much revenue do you get? (Answer: £10)
  • Write the calculation on the board: £0.50 × 20 = £10.00
  • Ask students: What about if you had costs of £3 to buy the ingredients – how much profit would you make? (Answer: £7)
  • Write the calculation on the board: £10.00 – £3.00 = £7.00
  • Click to reveal the questions on Slide 3.
  • Give students 1 minute to complete the two basic calculations. They should work individually, writing their answers in their exercise books.
  • Click on Slide 4 to reveal the answers.
  • Click through the slide to take students through the calculation of total revenue and profit. Address any issues with terminology, arithmetic or understanding.
  • Click on Slide 5. Tell students to copy down the formula for calculating market share into their exercise books.
  • Walk students through the example calculation illustrating how market share is calculated.
  • Click on Slide 6.
  • Give students 2 minutes to complete the practice calculations in their exercise books. They should work individually.
  • Click on Slide 7. With the calculation displayed, walk students through the method/answer. Repeat for Slide 8.
  • Click on Slide 9.
  • Select one student to come to the front and drag and drop to show their answers, and then check them. Ensure students are happy with the answers and resolve any questions.
  • If necessary, you could show the Key definitions again to help consolidate the new vocabulary for students. You could show Slide 2 again, as these are the five key financial objectives students need to take away and they will need to be sure of these for Task 1 in Student work 1 (you may wish them to make a note of these objectives in their exercise books if they have not already done so). Alternatively, or in addition, you could display the Key definitions on the board.

Task 1: Categorising financial objectives (4 minutes)

  • Display Question 1.
  • Explain to students: You need to identify which particular type of financial objective (from Teaching session 1) is represented by each example.
  • Give students two minutes to categorise each objective correctly. They should work individually.
  • Suggest students try to remember the five different categories but that they can use the notes they took earlier to help them if necessary.
  • Share answers as a class. Ask volunteers to give their answers and reasons for their choice.
  • Discuss any issues arising. For example, some students could see the ‘football kits’ as relating to sales and that could be correct. Ask for the justification and explain that often in business, a range of answers is possible, provided there is a valid justification for them.

Task 2: Worksheet 1 (10 minutes)

  • Hand out copies of Worksheet 1. Tell students: You are going to work in pairs and should read the instructions for both questions carefully.
  • As the first question on the worksheet (where students choose their own business) is not the primary focus of the task, stress that students should only spend a maximum of 4 minutes on this question. Tell them to focus on a small business that they are familiar with from their local area or that a family member works in. If students are taking too long to decide, give them the example of a small bakery on a local street to use.
  • For Question 2b, explain that the objectives should be realistic to the type and scale of the chosen business – for example, something that could be achieved by a relatively small business in its first year(s) of trading.
  • Ask volunteers to share their example objectives to check students’ understanding. If students cannot provide suitable suggestions, provide them from the answers on the answer sheet.

Support

For students who struggle with Financial objectives:

  • In Teaching session 1, some students may feel that objectives relating to market share could also relate to sales. This is true and so reassure students that, provided they give a suitable reason for the choice they have made, their answer would still be valid. Explain that, in a formal exam, questions such as Q1.4 that could potentially have either ‘sales’ or ‘market share’ as a possible answer would only have one of those options available for students to choose as their answer. Stress that examiners are not trying to ‘catch students out’ or trick them and so there would be no room for ambiguous answers that could create doubt in their minds about whether two answers could possibly be correct.
  • In Task 1 of Student work 1, some students may have difficulty classifying the business objectives. Suggest that they start by reviewing the notes they took of the five categories of financial objectives (survival, sales, profit, market share and financial security).
  • In Task 2 of Student work 1, some students may struggle to think of specific objectives of their own. It may help students if you show Question 1 from Task 1 of Student work 1 to provide some examples of the types of objective for each category.

Different styles of 1-mark question

  • Explain to students: Understanding the kind of response that different questions need is crucial. It will help you to answer questions as quickly and effectively as possible (and so score the best mark possible).
  • This section of the lesson will introduce students to different styles of 1-mark question.
  • Ask a volunteer: What does the key business term ‘objective’ mean? (Answer: A goal or target set by a business)
  • Tell students: We have just defined the term ‘objective’. This is a common type of exam question. It asks you to show your knowledge of key business terminology by providing a definition.
  • Explain that when students are asked to ‘define’ in an exam question, they should explain the meaning of the key word, trying to use precise business language. For certain key words, stating a formula will be sufficient. An example may be defining profit by using the formula ‘Total revenue – Total cost’. Students could also give an example in an answer to a ‘define’ question; giving an example will not be enough on its own but may help to convince an examiner if the student has not been completely accurate in the definition they have given.
  • Show the Key definitions to illustrate how much detail is required.
  • Give students 30 seconds to think about how they would answer the question ‘Define “Total revenue”.’ They could note down their ideas in their exercise books.
  • Ask a volunteer to provide an answer. (Possible answer: The money received from the sale of goods and services)
  • If the volunteer did not answer with a formula, ask the rest of the class if anyone answered the question by providing a formula and if so, to share it. (Answer: Total revenue = Selling price × Quantity sold)
  • Tell students: ‘Define’ is an example of a command word. A command word is the verb used in an exam question to explain what students need to do when giving their answer. In the exam, ‘Define’ questions are worth 1 mark.
  • Explain that another example of a command word is ‘give’.
  • Tell students: ‘Give’ questions want you to state a simple piece of information. They test knowledge and are also worth 1 mark. You usually only need to write one or two words to answer a ‘Give’ question.
  • Ask students to write an answer to the following question on mini whiteboards or pieces of paper: Give one example of a type of financial objective.
  • Tell students to hold up their mini whiteboards to share answers. (Possible answers: Profit; sales; survival; market share; financial security)
  • Tell students: This shows that you can score 1/1 with just one word for a ‘Give’ question, as long as you provide exactly the response needed.
  • Tell students: A third command word for a 1-mark question is ‘identify’. This requires you to understand information in a table or graph in order to provide the correct answer.
  • You could also explain that these three question types (define, give and identify) test the skill of ‘application’ because they have to use a given piece of information to find the correct answer. (The level of detail students require in an introduction here will probably depend on whether students in Y9 or earlier have been introduced to exam terms through other subjects.)
  • Display Question 2 and ask students to write their answer to the question on a mini whiteboard.
  • Ask students to hold up their whiteboards and check they’ve all got A as the correct answer before revealing the solution.
  • Briefly ask a student with the correct answer to explain how and why they chose it, to demonstrate rationale if anyone has got this incorrect.
  • Tell students: The final type of 1-mark question has the command word ‘State’. This requires you to combine your knowledge with application to a specific context that you have been given in the question or in a separate case study.
  • Show Slideshow: ‘State’ example.
  • Read the question aloud.
  • Click to reveal the example answer.
  • Ask a volunteer: Explain which part of the example answer shows relevant business knowledge. (Answer: 'financial security’)
  • Ask a volunteer: Explain which part of the example answer shows application. (Answer: ‘made Chris and Sarah Dawson some of the wealthiest business owners in the UK’)
  • Tell students: ‘State’ questions require you to use information given within the question (or a case study if this is provided) to give a ‘context’ for you to demonstrate your understanding of business. The company name will always be in italics to indicate that the question is testing application skills.
  • Ask students: What is another possible answer to the question about The Range that uses a different financial objective? (Possible answers: Sales revenue from selling household goods; profit from selling furniture such as tables; market share based on increasing the proportion of the UK’s household goods that it supplies.)
  • Time permitting, you could ask volunteers to explain which part of students’ answers to the question above illustrates knowledge and which application.
  • Hand out copies of Worksheet 2.
  • Give students 5 minutes to answer the questions in Section A. They should work individually.
  • Briefly share answers to Section A questions as a class. If necessary, display the Key definitions to support discussion relating to Question 3.
  • Students will tackle Section B in the End-of-lesson activity.

Support

For students who struggle with Different styles of 1-mark question:

  • In Teaching session 2, when considering Question 2, some students may not initially be able to identify the business with the largest market share from the pie chart. Explain that the entire circle of the pie chart represents the combined total of different, individual components. In this pie chart there are three components. Each coloured area represents the proportion of that total that each company has – in other words, their market share. So, the largest area belongs to the business with the largest market share.
  • You could use the class as an analogy to help further. For example, ask students: There are 30 students in the class. We can divide them into those with short hair and those with long hair. What would these groups look like represented as a pie chart? You could then draw a pie chart to show this representation.
  • In Teaching session 2 and Student work 2, some students may not be comfortable with the distinction between knowledge and application as exam skills. Explain that ‘knowledge’ relates to factual understanding. Ask them to think of the key terms from the lesson and illustrate that if, for example, they can define ‘Sales’, they are showing knowledge. If they can then find a word that has the same meaning as ‘sales’ or relates to the concept of sales, they are applying that understanding of the meaning of ‘sales’ to a piece of relevant information and so their answer is using their core knowledge and relating it to the outside world: the skill of application.
  • There is an example of this in Question 3 of Worksheet 2, where students are asked to identify the value of sales from a table and need to identify a word with the same meaning from the table and then quote the value of total revenue (or sales).
  • This activity follows on from Student work 1 and students will need to refer to their completed copies of Worksheet 1.
  • Hand out copies of Worksheet 3. This focuses on analysing the five financial objectives that students set for themselves as part of Worksheet 1 and reworking them to suit a larger, more established company. Students will then explain why the objectives differ from business to business.
  • Students can work with their partner from Student work 1 or individually.
  • Ask students to complete Question 6 (Section B) on Worksheet 2.
  • Give them 2 minutes to write their exam-style question and answer.
  • If necessary, remind students of the five financial objectives (survival, profit, sales, market share and financial security). You could briefly show again Slide 2 of Slideshow: Financial objectives of businesses from Teaching session 1.
  • Tell students to swap their exam-style question with a partner. Partners should attempt to answer the question.
  • Time allowing, invite volunteers to challenge the rest of their class with their exam-style question.
  • Finally, tell students that in the next lesson (Lesson 2), they will learn about a range of other objectives that companies may have.

Alternative activities

  • These resources can be used if you are splitting the content of this lesson into two sessions.
  • Give students one minute to think about what they want to achieve from studying business.
  • Invite two or three volunteers to share their thoughts. (Possible answers: To be able to start my own business; to get a good job)
  • Take one of the volunteers’ examples. Explain that this is their ‘objective’ in choosing to study business. Alternatively, you could explain that ‘to be able to start my own business’ is an objective in choosing to study business.
  • Tell students: Many people study business because they want to start their own company. Imagine you have started a business. How will you be able to tell if your business is succeeding?
  • Briefly discuss ideas, guiding students towards the idea that setting objectives is helpful when starting a business as this helps to identify a company’s key goals, and that focusing on these objectives could help a company to achieve those goals.
  • Tell students: The objectives of a business are the goals or targets it sets.
  • Ask students to write down an objective in their books that relates to what they want to achieve when studying International GCSE Business.
  • Revisit Worksheet 1.
  • Select a pair to share the overview of ‘their’ business (from Question 1 on the worksheet) with the class and then ask them to share two of their objectives (from Question 2 on the worksheet).
  • For each objective, ask the class to hold up mini whiteboards to show which of the five categories of financial objective is being described.
  • Ask a student who has the correct answer on their mini whiteboard to explain how they knew it belonged in their chosen category. For example, if an objective is ‘Have 2,000 subscribers to the website by the end of the year’ they might explain that this relates to ‘sales’ because each person who subscribes to the website is a customer and so represents a ‘sale’ of the company’s services.
  • Repeat this with further pairs if there is time.
