Lesson 1 | Overview Financial Aims and Objectives/Exam Skills 1

1.1.1
Businesses can have several objectives: financial aims and objectives – survival, profit, sales, market share and financial security.

In this lesson, students will learn to:

  • understand the different financial objectives of a business
  • answer different types of 1-mark exam questions.

As this is the first lesson of the course, most students will not have prior knowledge of this topic. However, they will be familiar with ideas relating to financial objectives such as selling things, earning money and being financially secure.

Subject vocabulary

  • entrepreneur: a person who takes risks and sets up a business
  • financial security: when a business earns enough money to both survive and satisfy the needs of the owner
  • market share: proportion of sales in a total market that a business or product enjoys
  • objectives: goals or targets set by a business
  • profit: when revenue is greater than costs (total revenue – total costs)
  • revenue: money from the sale of goods and services
  • sales volume: the number of products a business sells in a particular period of time
  • sales value: the worth of all the goods a business sells in a particular period of time (selling price × quantity sold)
  • survival: when a business makes enough money to cover the costs it has to pay in the next few months

General vocabulary

  • command word: the verb in an exam question that explains what a student is required to do
  • financial: relating to money

  • Some students may get confused about the meaning of ‘sales’ within a business context. Explain that ‘sales’ may also be used quite generally to talk about the volume of goods that are being sold. For example, ‘we want to increase our sales next year’ might just mean ‘we are going to sell more products next year than we did this year’. Slide 4 of the slideshow (‘Financial objectives of businesses’) in Teaching session 1 is a good opportunity to illustrate the difference between (sales) quantity or volume and (sales) revenue.
  • You could explain that it is always best, where possible, to use precise language in any academic subject and so it is advisable to use ‘sales revenue’ when talking about a certain value of sales. For example, ‘Apple sold goods worth $366 bn in 2021’ – it would be more precise to say, ‘Apple had a sales (or total) revenue of $366 bn in 2021’. This could be shortened to ‘Apple had sales of $366bn in 2021’.
  • Students may confuse profit and sales (revenue), forgetting that costs must be paid out of revenue before it can become profit. Take them through the definition of sales revenue and profit so they understand how they can be calculated (TR = SP × Q and Profit = TR – TC). Point out that calculating profit involves revenue so there is clear distinction between the two. Profit will always be less than the value of revenue. Encourage students to think of a very simple business that they are familiar with and to focus on that business’s costs, revenues and profits so that they can ‘anchor’ those concepts around a business they know well.
