Calculating percentages

  • Tell students: percentages are a useful way of calculating a rate of change to show how quickly things are improving (or getting worse). They will also tell a business how much profit they are making on each sale. The key percentage calculations that we need to understand are:
    • calculating percentage changes from figures in a graph or table
    • calculating a number as a percentage of another number.

  • Ask for a volunteer to explain how to calculate the percentage change in revenue from 2019 to 2020.
  • Use the student’s suggestion to write the formula onto the board:
    • \(\frac{(New\;value\;-\;Orginal\;Value)}{Orginal\;Value}\times100\)
    • Answer:\(\frac{(149-75)}{75}\times100=98.67\%\)
  • Tell students: This is an increase because the value is positive. If the value was negative, this would show a decrease.

Support

For students who struggle with Calculating percentages:

  • In Teaching session 1, some students may struggle with the concept of a percentage (or proportion). Use the example of students within a class: ask students to add up how many students from one particular tutor group there are in the Business class, add up the total number of all students in the class. Show that by dividing the number of tutor group members by the total number of students we are working out the proportion of students who fit into a specific category (and that we simply multiply the result by 100 to turn it into a percentage).
  • In Student work 1, some students may need support with identifying which numbers to ‘plug in’ to their percentage change formula in Q1. Use the initial example from Slide 1 of the slideshow in Teaching session 1 relating to revenue to exemplify that the ‘change’ is simply the difference between the ‘New’ and ‘Old’ values so is one minus the other and the ‘Old’ will always be the value before the change. Use the example from Slide 2 of the slideshow in Teaching session 1 relating to ‘cost of sales’ to walk students through identifying the ‘Change’ and Original.
  • In Student work 1, some students may struggle to start Q2 and Q3. Take them through the calculation of gross profit as this is likely to be what causes them a problem. Explain that the gross profit margin is the percentage of the sales revenue that the company keeps as gross profit and get them to calculate this. Then tell them that operating profit margin is 57.8% and get them to use the same methodology to calculate the operating profit value (Answer: £693,600) to see whether they have grasped the process. They can then check the £693,000 answer by subtracting expenses (given in Worksheet 1) from the gross profit figure.

Calculating averages

  • Tell students: Calculating averages is useful for businesses in many contexts. Averages are used to measure how well businesses perform in a number of different ways, such as how much (on average) it costs them to produce each product, how many customers (on average) each staff member serves in a day or how much revenue or profit they earn (on average) from each product sold.
  • Provide an example by asking a small number of students to state their shoe sizes and writing them on the board (e.g. 36, 42, 46, 37, 40 and 39).
  • Ask students: How would you calculate the average shoe size of this group of students? (Answer: By adding up the different values (shoe sizes of the group) and then dividing by the number of group members)
  • Perform the calculation on the board:
    • \(\frac{(36+42+46+37+40+39)}6=40\)
  • Tell students: This is what a company would do to calculate average values. For example, to calculate the average cost of producing each product, the company would add up all of the different costs involved in making the product and then divide that figure by the number of products made.
  • Click to show Slide 1 of the Slideshow: Calculating averages.
  • Give students 3 minutes to calculate the average cost for each of the companies shown. They should work individually, copying and completing the table in their exercise books.
  • Ask for volunteers to provide answers for company A’s total costs, company A’s average cost/unit, and so on etc. Click to reveal each answer one by one.
  • Tell students: A similar calculation you may have to perform is to calculate an average selling price or average revenue per unit. Average selling price and average revenue per unit are effectively the same thing. They can be calculated in exactly the same way as average cost/unit has just been calculated.
  • Click on Slide 2.
  • Give students 2 minutes to calculate the average revenue/unit for each company. They should work individually.
  • Ask students to show answers on mini whiteboards and go through them one at a time by clicking to reveal each answer.
  • Tell students: Another really useful application of averages for a business is calculating what is known as labour productivity. That is the average number of products each worker in the company produces in a day, month or year.
  • Click on Slide 3.
  • Tell students: To calculate labour productivity, we take the total level of output (number of products/units made) and divide that by the number of workers it took to produce that volume of goods.
  • Tell students to copy the formula from Slide 3 into their exercise books.
