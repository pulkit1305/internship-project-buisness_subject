Lesson 04: Overview

Calculating Percentages and Average

This lesson focuses on key quantitative skills. The quantitative skills addressed in this lesson are:
Calculation

Calculations in a business context:

  • percentages and percentage changes
  • averages
  • revenue, costs and profit.

Interpretation


Interpretation and use of quantitative data in business contexts to support, inform and justify business decisions:

  • information from graphs and charts
  • market data, including market share, changes in costs and changes in prices.

Students should already be familiar with the mathematical basis for the calculations they need to perform and the types of charts or tables they need to interpret from studying mathematics. This lesson focuses on practising those basic numeracy skills and allowing students to see how they need to apply those general skills within the context of a business generally and specifically in preparation for the International GCSE Business exam.

  • cost of sales: costs such as raw materials and the wages of factory workers
  • expenses: costs not directly linked to output, such as rental costs or interest paid on loans
  • gross profit formula: gross profit = sales revenue – cost of sales
  • operating profit formula: operating profit = gross profit – expenses
  • labour productivity: output per worker in a given time period.

In this lesson, students will each require a calculator.

  • Some students may not appreciate the distinction between ‘cost of sales’ and ‘sales’ or ‘total costs’. Use examples of types of cost of sale and expense to illustrate the difference between the different types of cost. For example, companies earn their revenue from selling goods and this is known as ‘sales’ but need to buy products that they can then sell to customers, so initially purchasing the goods is a ‘cost of sale’, whereas paying the electricity bill is an expense. Practice applying this knowledge to different questions is usually the best way to increase student confidence with this type of misconception.

  • When calculating percentage changes, some students may struggle to identify the correct denominator to use within the calculation. This problem will sometimes lead them to use the ‘new’ figure as the denominator rather than the ‘old’ one. Walking students through the calculation for Q3e of the Challenge activity (Worksheet 3) may help to provide an example of how to identify the ‘old’ figure: go through the percentage increase in total sales calculation with students and then get them to calculate the total costs to ensure they are using the year 1 figure as this relates nicely to the ‘old/new’ issue.
