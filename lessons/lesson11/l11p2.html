  • Ask a student: Which particular form of unincorporated business is being described? How do you know? (Answer: sole trader as it refers to just one owner and has unlimited liability; for example, a small building business.)

    • Incorporated business: public limited company, due to ability to sell shares on the stock exchange (for example, Nike).

The three sectors of the economy

  • Tell students: We are going to look at the classification of businesses today. In Business we place companies into one of three different ‘sectors’ depending on what their business activity is.
  • The first sector of the economy is the primary sector which involves extracting raw materials from the earth. Activity in this sector usually involves farming, fishing, forestry or mining.
  • Click on Slide 1 of the slideshow and click to reveal the first bullet.

Slideshow: Sectors of the economy

  • Tell students to make a note of the information in their exercise books.
  • Tell students: The main purpose of primary sector activity is to obtain raw materials. These raw materials may well not be used in the form they are extracted in. Instead, they are often processed into a finished product. This activity of turning a raw material into a finished good is what takes place in the secondary sector.
  • Click to reveal the definition of the secondary sector and tell students to make a note of the information in their exercise books.
  • Tell students:
    • There may well be different stages within the secondary sector. For example, a company which produces engine parts for jets is in the secondary sector and will sell those parts to a company such as Boeing who would use them to manufacture the finished plane. Both of the companies are in the secondary sector despite their supplier and customer relationship.
    • A chain of production refers to the stages which turn raw materials into components and then transform those components into finished goods that are ready for sale to consumers. The chain of production is what links the three sectors together and moves the product through its entire manufacture, moving closer to the consumer as it moves through the chain of production.
  • Click to reveal the definition of the tertiary sector and tell students to make a note of the information.
  • Tell students: The tertiary sector involves performing any type of service for consumers or selling any type of product to them. Explain that examples of tertiary activity include dentistry, retailing food, cleaning someone’s house, performing at concerts or working as a sports coach.
  • Click on Slide
  • Tell students they have 3 minutes to describe the chains of production which link the activities on the slide. They should work individually.
  • Tell students to make sure they clearly identify which sector of the economy each of the different processes belongs to.
  • Tell them: There are five different primary sector activities and each is associated with two different secondary sector activities which support one tertiary sector activity. You should start by identifying all of the primary sector activities and then, one-by-one, link the secondary and tertiary sector activities to each primary activity in turn.
  • Ask a volunteer to suggest a chain of production that they have identified.
  • Tell the remainder of the class to listen and raise their hands when activity moves from one sector to another.
  • Click on Slide 3 to reveal the answers.
  • Ask students to work in pairs. Give them 2 minutes to suggest two further chains of production for products or services of their choice. Ensure that they identify the different activities that take place in each stage of production.
  • Tell students: For example, to produce this lightbulb, tungsten is mined, which is a primary sector activity. That raw metal is then processed, stretched and twisted in a factory to turn it into a filament which forms part of the lightbulb and is a secondary sector activity along with assembling the filament into the finished bulb. Tertiary sector activity would involve using the lightbulb to illuminate a room where students are being educated or simply selling the lightbulb to consumers.
  • Ask for a pair to share one of their chains of production.
  • Repeat with another pair of students.
  • Tell students: All businesses within an economy will rely on other businesses to either supply them with raw materials or components or finished goods that they will use to deliver a service to their customers. This means that businesses are interdependent, because the activities of one business affect others within the economy because they are linked.

Changes in industrial sectors over time

  • Draw students' attention to the case study ‘Agricultural employment’ on p. 51 of the Student Book. (They have just looked at this as part of Student work 2.)
  • Ask students: What sector of the economy is the olive farm in and why? (Answer: primary, because it involves agriculture – growing a crop.)
  • Ask students: Do you think that more or fewer people are employed in agriculture now compared to 100 years ago? Make sure you have a reason for your answer before putting your hand up. Allow students to share ideas, guiding them to the correct answer. (Answer: Fewer people are employed in the agricultural sector now than 100 years ago because of automation, where many jobs in farming can now be done by machines rather than humans.)
  • Give an example to illustrate the answer: For example, the family in the case study have thought about investing in machinery to reduce the number of people, and therefore the labour costs, involved in harvesting the olives.
  • Tell students: This is true of most primary sector activity. For example, mining used to be very labour intensive, using just basic hand-held tools, but is now performed by huge, earth-moving machines. This has improved the efficiency of mining, allowing far fewer workers to produce a much greater total output.
  • Click on Slide 1 of the slideshow and tell students to make a note of the information.
Slideshow: Changes in industrial sectors over time
  • Tell students: Capital intensive means dominated by machines rather than humans. You should note this down in your exercise books
  • Click to reveal six changes in the primary sector.
  • Ask students: Which of these features are advantages and which are disadvantages?
  • Lead a class discussion. Ask students to put their hands up if they think ‘increased efficiency’ is an advantage rather than a disadvantage and ask a volunteer to explain why. Repeat for the other features.
  • Possible answers:
    • Increased efficiency is an advantage because it allows businesses to reduce the average cost of growing crops or extracting oil, meaning that those goods are cheaper for consumers.
    • Lower employment is a disadvantage because there are fewer jobs available to the population, meaning incomes and living standards may fall.
    • Reduced costs of production is an advantage as it allow businesses to increase the profits they make.
    • Greater investment required can be a disadvantage because it makes it harder for small, family-run businesses to compete with bigger rivals.
    • Work being much safer is an advantage because it reduces the risk of injury or death for workers.
    • Greater total output is an advantage as it allows more finished goods to be created, improving living standards and allowing populations to grow.
  • Tell students: Industrialisation has meant that over the last 200 years the secondary sector has increased in size. The development of large-scale machinery allowed big companies to be created which revolutionised our society, giving us access to many new types of good and allowing us to buy better quality and much cheaper products of many different types.
  • Ask students: Do you think the process of industrialisation is still continuing within our country?
  • Give students 1 minute to discuss their opinions with a partner. Tell them they will need to be able to justify their views.
  • Invite volunteer pairs to share their ideas and justify them.
  • Tell students:
    • In many countries, a process of de-industrialisation has been taking place in recent years, where economic activity has moved from the secondary to the tertiary sector. This has occurred because many countries found that it was cheaper to buy manufactured goods from other countries where labour costs were lower. Therefore, the secondary sector in more-economically developed countries has declined over the last 50 years.
    • In some developing countries, for example, Vietnam and Bangladesh, the secondary sector has grown because of this trend.

  • Tell students: Wages in the secondary sector have traditionally been higher (and employment more stable) than in primary sector industries. Therefore, changes in the structures of the economy have tended to be accompanied by rising living standards as those economies industrialised. More economically developed countries have recently been going through a process of de-industrialisation, where tertiary sector activity has become dominant.

  • Use the tertiary sector examples to explain:
    • Many occupations within the tertiary sector tend to involve providing a service and involve the highest levels of skill within our economy. This means that the incomes generated and profits earned in this sector are high and many countries have sought to develop their tertiary sectors. For example, China has sought to develop its tourist industry as well as banking services and technology services because of the growth and revenue-earning opportunities these industries offer.

Answers

2              The pie charts in Figure 7.3 show some important changes in the amount of employment in different sectors of the economy between 1990 and 2015. In 1990, China employed 60 per cent of its workforce in agriculture. By 2015, this had fallen to just 28.3 per cent. In contrast, the number of people employed in the tertiary sector rose from 18 per cent to 42.4 per cent. Employment in the secondary sector also grew from 22 per cent to 29.3 per cent. These changes have resulted in a more balanced economy for China. It was less dependent on agriculture in 2015 as it was in 1990. However, if the changes follow the same trend, China’s economy may become more unbalanced in the future as it becomes more dependent on services.

3              Businesses in each of the three sectors rely on each other. For example, in the primary sector cereal farmers rely on bakers in the secondary sector for their sales of wheat. Bakers may depend on advertising agencies in the tertiary sector to produce television adverts for their products. The transport industry in the tertiary sector relies on the oil industry in the primary sector to provide fuel for its vehicles. Workers in both the secondary and tertiary sectors rely on the primary sector for their food. This reliance between sectors is called interdependence. In this case, Hisense may rely on retailers and other distributors to sell its manufactured goods to consumers.

4              De-industrialisation occurs in well-developed countries, such as the USA, Germany, Japan and France. It involves the decline of the manufacturing sector at the expense of the tertiary sector. In the last 20 or 30 years, many western-style economies have seen employment in manufacturing fall sharply. However, at the same time there has been a steady increase in the quantity and value of services.

5              A number of suggestions have been suggested to explain the process of de-industrialisation. There may have been a change in consumer demand. People may prefer to spend more of their income on services rather than manufactured goods. For example, people may be spending more on holidays, restaurants, travelling and personal services and less on consumer durables. Competition in manufacturing may be another important reason. Recently there has been some fierce competition in the production of manufactured goods from developing countries such as India, China, Thailand and Brazil. These countries can often produce manufactured goods more cheaply than in western economies. In this case, China has seen an increase in the manufacturing sector. In the last 30 years or so, China has developed a competitive edge in this sector due mainly to its cheap and large supply of labour.

Another reason is that as countries become more developed the public sector tends to grow. Since the public sector mainly provides services such as education and transport and healthcare, this adds to the growth of the tertiary sector. Finally, advances in technology mean that employment in manufacturing falls because machines replace people. The reason for the decline in manufacturing in well-developed countries may be different depending on the circumstances of the individual nation. For example, a country that has an inefficient manufacturing sector may be vulnerable to overseas competition. In this case, China, which is still a developing country (although it is developing rather rapidly), has seen an increase in the tertiary sector from 18 per cent to around 42 per cent. This trend is likely to continue in the future as the country develops – the tertiary sector will grow as it has done in developed nations.
