Lesson 11 | Overview Classification of Businesses

1.3.1
Primary, secondary and tertiary activities:
  • primary sector – extracting raw materials from the earth
  • secondary sector – converting raw materials into finished or semi-finished goods
  • tertiary sector – provision of a wide variety of services.

These quantitative skills are also addressed in this lesson:

Calculation

Calculations in a business context:

  • percentages

Interpretation

Interpretation and use of quantitative data in a business context to support, inform and justify business decisions:

  • information from graphs and charts

In this lesson, students will learn to:

  • distinguish between the three main sectors of the economy
  • understand the changes that have occurred in sectors over time.

Students are not expected to have any knowledge of this topic, as it is new learning for the International GCSE course. However, many students will have studied Geography and so may have an understanding of the different sectors into which businesses are classified. (See Common errors and misconceptions for a potential issue this can cause.)

Subject vocabulary

  • capitasl intensive: production activity that is dominated by machinery rather than by using human labour
  • chain of production: the stages involved in turning raw materials into finished goods for sale to consumers
  • de-industrialisation: an economy moving away from manufacturing activity, usually towards the tertiary sector
  • labour intensive: production which involves more human inputs relative to machinery
  • primary sector: extracting raw materials from the earth
  • secondary sector: converting raw materials into finished or semi-finished goods
  • tertiary sector: providing a wide variety of services

General vocabulary

  • industrialisation: a process where an economy moves away from primary sector activity towards a greater focus on manufacturing activity

No equipment is needed for this lesson.

  • Many students will have been introduced to a classification of business sectors in their Geography studies and, as a result, some students will sub-divide the tertiary sector into tertiary (consumer services) and quaternary (professional services) sectors. This distinction is not recognised as part of the business course, so tell students to ignore any such distinction and simply think of any services (retail, research or household and so on) as being part of the tertiary sector.
