  • Worksheets will need to be printed for students in advance of the lesson.
  • While all care is taken to ensure resource content is in line with cultural sensitivities, please be aware that it should be reviewed before sharing with students.

Internal factors on business objectives

  • Tell students: Business objectives are likely to change over time and today’s lesson focuses on some of the reasons for this. First, we will focus on internal causes of change.
  • Tell them: ‘Internal’ refers to factors that are specific to one particular business and so will not necessarily affect other businesses within the industry.
  • Display Question 2.
  • Ask students to think (individually) about the types of financial objective they would set for each of the years. After 1‒2 minutes, ask for volunteers to suggest an objective for each year and to give a brief reason.
  • Probable answers:

    • Reason

    2017

    Survival

    Because the business is new and expects a higher level of costs than revenues, it would be delighted simply to make it through the first year.

    2018

    Growth/survival – break even/sales

    The expectation that there will be no need for loans infers that the business will break even. Growth is also hinted at via the increasing customer numbers.

    2019

    Sales/profit

    Growth in demand for the business’s products implies that it may well set a ‘sales’-based objective.

    2020

    Market share/profit

    Having a product which is judged to be superior to its competitors’ might allow it to ‘steal’ customers from them and so increase its market share.

    2021

    Profit/financial security

    The owner’s personal financial objectives are hinted with a thought of retirement based on the business’s success and so financial security may be considered.
  • Draw students’ attention to how the business’s objectives changes over time. Explain that the stage of development of a business is an example of an internal factor that affects its objectives.
  • Tell students: As businesses change and evolve over time, so do their objectives. Amazon focused on growth for the first 10 years of its existence, during which it made losses in order to increase its market share. Once it had become market leader in a number of markets, it changed its focus to objectives relating to profit.
  • Tell them: Changes in the size of a business may also lead their objectives to change. Small businesses have less than 50 employees while large businesses have more than 250.
  • Ask students: Can you suggest why the size of a business might affect its objectives? Students may find it useful to think about specific businesses they are familiar with. For example a small business local to them and a large business they have bought goods from. Discuss students’ ideas. (Possible answers:
    • Small companies may sell just a tiny proportion of the total sales in a market so could be unlikely to focus on market share. Market share may be important for the largest companies in an industry.
    • Small companies may be owned by just one person and so have their owner’s personal financial security as a key objective, but larger companies are often owned by many different shareholders who will each focus on the company being as profitable as possible.)
  • Explain that another internal factor that might affect objectives is when the owner or leader changes. Examples could be:
    • If a new Chief Executive takes over, they may have a different idea about how to achieve long-term success for the company and so change objectives (profit to sales).
    • If a founder with an objective related to social objectives sells the company to a different owner, the new owners may focus instead on profits.
  • Tell students: The performance of a company may also mean that there is a need to change its objectives. For example, a company pursuing a sales or growth objective was successful in increasing the volume of goods sold. However, this has led to falling profits because they cut prices to sell more. They might decide that now that customers have tried their products and hopefully liked them, the time is right to increase prices and so focus on becoming more profitable. As a real example, Netflix kept price rises to a minimum for the first 8 years of its streaming service as it grew its user base and achieved their market share and sales objectives. It then increased the price of its standard service in the US from $10.99 to $15.49 within 4 years.

Task 2: Case study: SuperSkiSwiss.com (8 minutes)

  • Students should read the case study about SuperSkiSwiss.com on p. 18 of the Student Book, then answer Chapter question 6 on the same page.
  • They should write two paragraphs for their answer, explaining how a company like SuperSkiSwiss.com might change its objectives as it evolves.
  • Give them 6 minutes to complete this task. They should work individually.
  • When they have finished, re-group as a class. Ask a volunteer to share their answers, focusing on the examples they have chosen. Use their examples to discuss briefly how companies are likely to have objectives focused more on survival/growth in their early years but as they evolve or become larger they frequently become more focused on profits.

Answer

How might a company like SuperSkiSwiss.com change its objectives as it evolves? Provide two reasons in your analysis.

Over a period of time, as businesses like SuperSkiSwiss.com evolve, aims and objectives are likely to change. This is usually because businesses have to respond to certain events or changes in circumstances. For example, businesses like SuperSkiSwiss.com operate in dynamic markets. This means they have to deal with regular changes. For example, a new entrant may appear in the market, a rival might introduce a new product or the economy may start to decline. When market conditions change it may be necessary to set new objectives. In this case the fashion industry is highly competitive. For example, if trading becomes difficult as a result of more intense competition, SuperSkiSwiss.com may decide that survival is more important than rapid growth until the market 'settles down'.

New legislation might also have an impact of the objectives of a business. In recent years many business have become more socially responsible. This may be a reaction to new environmental, employment or consumer legislation.

In this case, SuperSkiSwiss.com claims that it wants to grow in a sustainable way. As a result, for example, the businesses is committed to the protection of the environment. This might be the result of a growing body of legislation passed to help protect the environment. SuperSkiSwiss.com aims to use resources as efficiently as possible by controlling emissions, and focusing on the sustainability of packaging. In 2016/17 SuperSkiSwiss.com cut its carbon emissions by 15 per cent. It also reduced its stock and vehicle movements across Europe and saved 11 tonnes of materials by reducing the number of swing tags (product labels) used. These results suggest that SuperSkiSwiss.com may have modified its objectives as a result of environmental legislation.

Finally, the reasons for a business changing its objectives are often due to external factors. These are factors which are beyond the control of businesses. However, sometimes a business such as SuperSkiSwiss.com might change its objectives for internal reasons. For example, there may be change in the senior management team. In such circumstances the objectives might change. For example, new senior managers might want to pursue profit maximisation so that higher dividends can be paid to shareholders. However, there is no evidence in this case to suggest that SuperSkiSwiss.com has made any such changes.

  • In Student work 1, Task 2 some students may struggle to work out how to structure a response to Chapter question 6 on p. 18 of the Student Book. They may then be able to think about how those factors may have influenced SuperSkiSwiss’s objectives. (Those objective changes may be quite similar to those from Worksheet 1, Task 1.)

How external factors influence a business’s objectives

  • Tell students: We will now be focusing on how external factors influence objectives. External factors are those that the business cannot control and are, therefore, likely to affect all businesses in an industry.
  • Display Slide 1 of Slideshow: External factors influencing business objectives.
  • Tell students: Three main categories of external factors affect businesses’ objectives.
  • Read the categories through as a class.
  • Tell students: Legislation refers to a law or set of laws.
  • Click on Slide 2. Give students 1 minute to consider, individually, whether the examples of the influence of different factors on a company are classified as relating to market conditions, technological change or new legislation.
  • Go through the examples one-by-one. Ask students to show how they have classified each one using mini-whiteboards (they should write an M for market conditions, T for technological change or L for legislation).
  • Click on Slide 3 to reveal the answers. Tell students: China’s ban on fossil-fuelled cars may lead some car companies to focus on growth objectives rather than profit ones. This might be because they spend much more for the next 5 years on research into battery technology and electric cars with the intention of launching new models to fit with the change in the law. This might lead to a return to a sales or profit objective when they are ready to launch the new models of car.
  • Click to reveal an instruction which asks students to think (individually) for 1 minute of their own examples of how the external factors above might affect a company or industry.
  • Ask for volunteers to explain their idea about how the changes would affect a company’s objectives. (Possible answers:
    • Market conditions: the recession might lead luxury goods companies to switch away from objectives relating to market share and to focus instead on survival as their customers are likely to focus more on buying necessities like food rather than luxury goods.
    • Technological change: the advances in battery capacity might lead to a large increase in the number of customers looking to buy solar panels for their homes/businesses. Therefore, companies producing them might change their objectives from profit to sales or growth.
    • Legislation: service stations which sell petrol fuel may change objectives from profit to survival as they have to invest a lot of money to cope with the changing law that means they need to install lots of new charging points for electric vehicles.)
  • Click on Slide 4.
  • Give students 2 minutes to discuss with a partner another example of an external factor within each of the three categories and to explain how it might affect the objectives of a business.
  • If students struggle, give them an example for legislation: A new law that limits the price that energy suppliers can charge their customers for electricity might reduce the number of companies that want to join that industry. Companies already supplying electricity might have to change their objectives from profit to sales so that they can stay in business by offering the lowest price and so gaining extra customers.
  • Share ideas as a class. (Possible answers:
    • Legislation: the EU brought in a law stating that all electrical devices could be returned for a full refund if they became faulty within 2 years of purchase. This could force some suppliers of cheaper devices to switch from focusing on sales (by offering low prices for low quality goods) and instead look to improve quality and focus on surviving whilst they develop longer-lasting products.
    • Market conditions: the increasing incomes of Chinese consumers might lead companies who previously focused on being profitable in European markets to switch to a growth strategy and to try to enter the Chinese market so that they obtain more sales or market share in that country.
    • Technological change: the advent of the internet has allowed companies to market their products to consumers (about whom they know a lot through monitoring their browsing history) at very low costs and to achieve more sales. This might allow companies to move from focusing on survival to be able to focus on sales or profit objectives.)
  • Tell students: Technological change happens continuously and often presents opportunities; for example, Netflix initially rented and sold films on DVD through the post, but the advent of streaming meant this became unprofitable so the company changed from focusing on sales to an objective based on developing its own content and allowing users to stream it. It also often presents threats; for example, Blockbuster in the UK could not cope with the advent of streaming and so went out of business.
  • Explain that technological change does not just affect the product a company sells, but how it sells it (over the internet, as a virtual product rather than a physical one (computer games)) and also how it makes it.
  • Click on Slide 5.
  • Tell students: Technological progress allows better machines to be created which can produce larger quantities of products more quickly. This is linked to an economy of scale (the advantage a company can receive from selling greater quantities of goods) where larger companies can afford more expensive machinery that allows them to produce their products in far greater quantities and at much lower costs. These companies are likely to also practise automation, where they replace human workers with machines and so save money on wages.
  • Give students 3 minutes to discuss in pairs about how automation and economies of scale might affect either the company that is automating production or its rivals who do not benefit from economies of scale. Ask for volunteers to share their answers and discuss as a class. (Possible answers:
    • The largest car companies can experience economies of scale where they can install robots that work 24 hours/day to assemble parts into a finished car, meaning the company can produce far more cars every day than if it relied on humans to assemble the car. This might mean that in the short term the company might focus on survival while it pays for installing the automated production but allow it to then follow a growth objective before finally benefitting by focusing on profit due to the lower costs it has once the robots have been paid for.
    • A small car company which relies on hand-building sports cars might find it now appears much more expensive than its larger rivals which have been automated. This could force it to abandon objectives relating to market share or sales and to focus just on survival.)
  • Hand out Worksheet 2 and tell students to complete Question 1 individually. They have 3 minutes.
  • Stress that they are choosing the business that is most (or least) negatively impacted by the pandemic (i.e. who suffered the worst) rather than just who might have been significantly impacted but not in a bad way (e.g. Zoom was heavily affected but in a good way so you would choose ‘least’ for it).
  • After 3 minutes, tell students to complete the remaining three questions on the worksheet with a partner. They have 10 minutes.
  • Briefly share and discuss some of the students’ answers as a class, focusing on answers to Question 4.

Support

For students who struggle with How external factors influence a business’s objectives:

  • In Teaching session 2, some students may struggle with understanding an economy of scale. Get them to think about how much it costs to buy a single can of Coca Cola from a small shop (£1) and then ask if they know how much a 12-can pack might cost from a supermarket (12 cans for £6 and so only 50p each). So it is cheaper to buy in bulk and applied to a business, shows that buying materials in bulk illustrates a financial benefit of being large.
  • In Student work 2, some students may struggle to get started with Question 4. They may benefit from an example to help them understand the requirements of the task or how to approach it. Use an example (from Worksheet 2 Answers) to give them a model of what each of the three question parts (a–c) requires, then suggest that they focus on each of the three parts in succession. For example:
    • a) Peloton saw a large increase in the number of people interested in keeping fit at home as they could not go to gyms.
    • b) Therefore, its objective changed from being solely focused on growth to also include increasing profits.
    • c) Peloton added lots of new instructors to the platform to provide users with a greater range of exercise classes and so appeal to as wide a range of people as possible and ensure that more subscribers signed up.

They can use the answers to Question 1 to help them understand how the company was affected and then their notes from the last two lessons (Lessons 1 and 2) about what different objectives are available to companies, before being free to use their creativity in suggesting answers for the final part of the question.
