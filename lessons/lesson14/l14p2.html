Recapping influences on location

  • Tell students: Last lesson (Lesson 13), we looked at some of the factors that influence the location of businesses.
  • Ask students: What are these factors?
  • As students volunteer the factors, list them on the board:
    • proximity to market
    • materials
    • competitors
    • labour
    • the nature of business activity (which sector of the economy the business is in).
  • Divide students into groups of three or four.
  • Hand out Worksheet 1.
  • Assign a different ‘business’ from the worksheet to each group of students.
  • Tell each group to sit together.
  • Alternatively, you could ask students to use the businesses they planned and ‘set up’ during Lesson 5 and Lesson 6 for this activity. They should have notes on that business in their exercise books from Lesson 5 to remind them of the nature of ‘their’ business. If they do not, assign them one of the businesses on the worksheet.
  • Give students 1 minute to consider question 2 individually. Remind them to look at the list of factors on the board, particularly for part c.
  • After 1 minute, give students 90 seconds to discuss their priorities with the other owners of ‘their’ business (question 3).
  • Ask a representative from one of the groups to present their priorities and the reasonsq behind them.
  • Ask a representative from a group with a contrasting set of priorities to explain them.
  • Ask students to raise their hands if their business was in the primary, secondary and finally tertiary sector. The majority will be tertiary sector busineses. (Answers: Novas tertiary; Harpers tertiary, PM Academy tertiary, The Herald primary, Worzels primary, A piece of cake tertiary, iCan secondary, Cre8if FX tertiary)

The impact of the internet on business location

  • Tell students to think about the business they were assigned in the Starter
  • Ask them to raise their hands if they think the internet and the e-commerce opportunities it creates would have a significant influence on where they might decide to locate their business.
  • Ask a student who has their hand raised: Can you explain how or why the internet affects your business’s choice of location? (Possible answers: Do not need a shop to sell from, many staff can work from home, ‘physical’ products have been replaced by ‘virtual’ ones)
  • Display Slide 1 of the slideshow.
  • Ask students to work in their groups from the Starter activity and give them 1 minute to think about where they would place their business on the ‘map’ shown.
  • Give each group a sticky note and tell them to write their company’s name on it.
  • Invite group representatives to come up to the front one at a time and place it on the appropriate place on the displayed ‘map’. If the slideshow does not display large enough, draw the map on the board.
  • As students come up to the front, ask a couple of them to explain what their business does and why they feel the internet will have the anticipated level of impact.
  • Next, tell students that you are writing the names of other businesses on sticky notes and that they will have to tell you where to position that sticky note on the ‘map’.
  • Write ‘Walmart’ on a sticky note. If necessary, explain that Walmart is the world’s largest supermarket chain.
  • Ask students: Where should I place Walmart on the ‘map’?
  • Encourage students to call out and use ‘up/down/left/right’ to come to an agreed position: Walmart should sit in the tertiary area and may be somewhere around the centre line as they still sell the majority of goods through physical stores.
  • Ask a volunteer who was particularly vocal regarding the positioning: Why does Walmart belong here? Can you justify why the location is or is not influenced by the internet?
  • Possible answers:
    • Low internet influence: need physical location to be near customers and transport/car parks and workers.
    • High internet influence: online grocery ordering means warehouses and distribution centres have to be carefully planned, increasingly offers banking and insurance services which can be delivered online.
  • Repeat the sticky note process for Prime Grain, Australia’s biggest wheat farmer. Prime Grain would be positioned on the bottom-left of the ‘map’.
    • Low internet influence: need physical location to be near materials and resources, need to be close to skilled labour.
    • High internet influence: not influenced in terms of location – however, internet will give better access to information about weather, market prices etc.
  • Repeat the process for Wedgewood, a manufacturer of pottery including dinner plates and bowls. Wedgewood is a secondary sector business but students may debate the degree of internet influence.
    • Low internet influence: need physical location to be near materials (clay etc.) and resources, need to be close to skilled labour. Also need to be close to competitors as they are based in ‘the Potteries’, an area of the UK known to be home to the country’s pottery industry.
    • High internet influence: 3D printing can revolutionise parts of the business, allowing anyone with access to a printer costing £200-400 to ‘print off’ their designs from home and so to be able to deliver designs to anywhere in the world, so the location must be in an area with sufficient internet access.
  • Repeat the process for Mulholland Automotive, a producer of parts used by F1 teams in their racing cars. Mulholland is in the secondary sector and could be positioned wherever students feel is most justified.
    • Low internet influence: need to be close to customers and key workers.
    • High internet influence: internet technology has revolutionised transport and delivery and so the business could deliver parts to customers within a matter of hours using IT systems, may be able to secure access to staff who work from home and collaborate on projects remotely, given the level of technology used in the design and manufacture of components, physical location may have decreased in importance.
  • Repeat the process for Deloitte, a company of accountants and management consultants employing 20,000 people in the UK. Deloitte should be in the top-right.
    • Low internet influence: need to be accessible to skilled labour, workers usually travel to visit their clients.
    • High internet influence: many staff can work remotely/from home, lots of customers can be serviced online, much of their client delivery is done by travelling to customers.
  • If students are not able to come up with many good reasons why Deloitte’s location has been heavily influenced by the internet, explain as follows:
    • Following the COVID-19 lockdowns, Deloitte realised that it could save large amounts of money if it had fewer or smaller offices. This was because it could allow the majority of its staff to do their jobs from home. Therefore, expensive offices that had been created in London to attract high-skilled workers living around the city could be closed or be much smaller.
    • Also, the Head Office did not need to be located in one place any longer, because better video conferencing and other online communications technologies allowed staff to meet virtually rather than having to be in one large expensive office.
    • Being able to offer home-working meant that the company could recruit the best staff wherever they lived. Customers had never really visited Deloitte offices, with staff visiting their clients instead and so the ‘close to market’ influence was not really relevant to where Deloitte chose to locate.  
  • Tell students: Look at the distribution of sticky notes across the board. Is there a pattern? Can you describe it?
  • Lead a brief discussion, guiding students towards the answer: It is likely that the degree to which the internet influences businesses will increase as we move from the primary to the tertiary sector. Therefore, the sticky notes are likely to be in a sweep from bottom-left to top-right.
  • Take a photo of the board with the sticky notes in case you want to refer back to it.
  • Click on Slide 2. (You will need to remove the sticky notes from the board.)
  • Tell students to make a note of the information.
  • Walk students through the points on the slide: There are a number of reasons why many businesses, especially those in the tertiary sector, may change their location, called relocating, based on the development of the internet and the improved connectivity it allows:
    • The growing trend towards internet shopping means operating from a physical store is not the only way of selling goods
    • Locating close to good transport links may be increasingly important for businesses who take online orders
    • Where to situate warehouses, logistics or distribution centres is increasingly important to businesses who send products to customers rather than operating physical stores
    • Many businesses can deliver their service remotely and so do not need any physical premises
    • Businesses can save money by reducing office spaces when staff work remotely and communicate by email, phone and video.
  • To round of this teaching session, display the key definitions.
  • Explain that a footloose business is one which can change its location quickly, rather than having to operate from a fixed location. Students will need to be aware of this term as they work on Student work 1.

You should then try and answer question 1 as though it were two separate three-mark questions. Try and discipline yourselves to only spend three minutes on each part.

The impact of government on business location 

  • Tell students:
    • Governments have a huge impact on where companies locate or choose to relocate their business or parts of the operations of larger businesses.
    • One way in which governments influence business location is through legal controls: these are rules, laws, payments, taxes or actions that affect how attractive or expensive it is to locate in a certain place or whether it is attractive, unattractive or impossible.
  • Ask students: Who can give me an example of a legal control that makes it less likely or impossible for a business to locate exactly where it chooses in its local town or city?
  • Possible answers:
    • Planning permission may be required to construct a new building, change the purpose of an existing business or to extend or expand a building
    • Licences may be required for businesses wishing to sell or produce certain goods (dangerous chemicals, food etc.)
    • Polluting industries may be restricted, limited or banned due to the effect they have on air or water quality, noise or light pollution etc.
    • Further development may be restricted due to congestion on roads or other transport networks.
  • Explain any of the above ‘restrictions’ which are not suggested.
  • Click on Slide 1 of the slideshow and tell students to make a note of the information.
  • Ask students: Why might governments be keen to attract more businesses to locate in their country (or a certain area of it)?
  • Possible answers:
    • To boost employment in a particular area
    • Because producing in their country creates tax revenues for the government
    • To bring new investment/ideas/skilled workers to a country/area
    • To create important new services for residents.
  • Click on Slide 2.
  • Tell students: Governments often try to attract new businesses to locate or relocate in a particular area. Some reasons for this are given are given on the slide.
  • Go through the list on the slide as a class.
  • Give students 2 minutes to discuss the question on the slide with a partner. If necessary, explain that to incentivise means to make something worthwhile. Tell them that they should come up with at least one suggestion for how each of the points could be achieved by a government.
  • Ask students pairs to suggest a method of attracting businesses to increase employment in an area.
  • Repeat for the three other ‘reasons’ on Slide 2.
  • Answers:
    1. offer training courses, provide subsidies for education etc., cut employment taxes, improve public transport
    2. provide infrastructure or amenities, reduce taxes, provide subsidies
    3. remove need for work permits, provide tax breaks on investment, promote the country to businesses/investors
    4. provide subsidies to businesses for building roads/links etc., create infrastructure for businesses to use (e.g. build sports facilities for private businesses to run).
  • Click on Slide 3.
  • Tell students: Infrastructure can refer to any systems that connect businesses and or people together, such as bridges, broadband links or roads.
  • Explain: Governments are keen to attract businesses who bring jobs, investment, skills, tax revenue and possibly new infrastructure to an area.
  • Go through the list of methods on the slide as a class and tell students to make a note of the key information. Mention that key infrastructure will include building roads, schools and 5G networks. Key activities may include providing payments to businesses for doing certain things e.g. investing in modern machinery or research, hiring unemployed workers.
  • Next, tell students:
    • A trade bloc is an association between nations who think that it will be beneficial for them to reduce the barriers to businesses and consumers trading goods and services between those member countries. The countries are often in the same area of the world.
    • Some of the ways trade blocs make doing business between members easier include reducing or removing tariffs, which are taxes on imported goods, reducing the level of paperwork or checks before moving goods between countries, allowing workers to work in any country, and bringing areas such as tax, labour laws etc. closer to a common standard. They may also align product standards. For example, all EU countries will use the same power cable to charge any electrical devices from
  • Click on Slide 4.
  • Tell students: The impacts are the benefits the features are intended to create. These benefits are incentives for businesses to relocate.
  • Give students 2 minutes to complete the matching activity individually. Tell students: Some impacts may result from more than one ‘feature’. If this is the case, prioritise the most important feature that creates that impact.
  • Tell students to hold up the answer to ‘A. Reduced tariffs’ on a mini whiteboard or a piece of paper (they can just write the number of the relevant impact).
  • Confirm the answer is impact 4.
  • Tell students: SAFTA is a trade bloc in South Asia where India, Pakistan and other countries in the area agreed to an initial 30% cut in tariffs on the movement of goods between countries with the objective of eventually removing all tariffs.
  • Repeat for remaining features, confirming answers and providing examples.
  • Feature B, Impact 1. Tell students: Border checks take a long time and reduce the attractiveness of international trade. For example, the CEMAC trade bloc in central Africa retains quite a lot of barriers and checks. This means 9 forms need to be filled out for cross-border trade, taking an average of 35.2 days to organise an export. The average among developed nations is 4.4 forms and 10.9 days. This shows why the reduction of trade barriers can provide an incentive to businesses to locate in a particular country because they will be able to trade internationally much more quickly, cheaply and easily.
  • Feature C, Impact 5. Tell students: All businesses supplying goods in the EU use the same electrical voltages and so goods sold in any country will work in any of the other 26 members, whereas an expensive transformer would be required to use a TV sold in the USA. If a producer of TV sets wants to sell to Europe, they may choose to locate in the EU rather than in the USA because they can sell the same good to 500 million consumers and so benefit from economies of scale.
  • Feature D, Impact 3. Tell students: Rules on managing a business will be uniform within a trade bloc. This will encourage businesses that want to expand out of a ‘home’ country to do so into another member of the bloc because the business will be able to operate in a way it is more comfortable with (compared to a non-member of the bloc where rules and laws may be very different).
  • Feature E, Impact 2. Tell students: Businesses can easily transfer workers between countries because the working conditions, contracts and worker protections are similar in other members of a trading bloc. Workers may also be able to move to countries within the bloc without any restriction.
  • Click on Slide 5 to see the answers.
  • Summarise by telling students: Membership of trade blocs provides a range of benefits to businesses. Remember that how easy it is to do business in a particular country is a key factor when businesses are deciding where to locate a new (part of their) business. Similarly, any factors which reduce the cost of doing business will also attract those businesses to locate within a trade bloc.

Answers

An example, using the fictional ‘Top Tips’ nail and manicure salon:

As a relatively small nail and manicure treatment salon, I do not have the option to move to a different country, but I could decide not to operate from a physical ‘salon’ if it would be more beneficial to allow customers to visit a salon in a room in my house or to go to customers’ homes or workplaces.

  • The government is considering a new law that means all tattoo and piercing salons have to have a special licence. If they extend this to beauty salons we would have to undertake training and pay for the licence application.
  • My local council may offer us reduced rates (the tax paid to local government based on a building’s value) because there are a number of empty shops in the area and they will want to keep a lot of customers coming to the area to encourage businesses. If they cannot give me financial support it strengthens the argument to ‘go mobile’. The government is offering a subsidy to reduce the costs of heating commercial buildings over the winter and so this increases the attractiveness of keeping a physical location.
  • The rent on my 41 m2 shop is £13,000/year, but if I paid about £10,000 I could convert a room in my house and never have to pay rent again. Similarly, I could use my car to travel to customers’ houses which would also cost nothing other than cost of petrol.
  • As the UK has left the EU trade bloc, I may struggle to attract the staff I would need to help me expand. This is because unemployment here is quite low and previously I could recruit staff from any country in the EU. However, if I can show I cannot find any British staff qualified to work for me, I might be able to bring in a trained technician from outside the EU (where many of those workers are currently located) and I would not have been able to when the UK was part of the EU.
  • On balance I think there are sufficient advantages of retaining a physical high street location to attract customers. Therefore, the government’s actions will not convince me to move in the way that some companies might do. However, given that most impacts of government action have a negative impact on my physical business, it might make me more likely to experiment with also offering my services on a mobile basis, rather than from a fixed location.
