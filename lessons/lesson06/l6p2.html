Recapping sole traders and partnerships

  • Explain to students that this activity gives them the chance to recap their knowledge of unincorporated businesses from the last lesson (Lesson 5).
  • Explain why this is important: Many of the features of businesses we looked at in the last lesson are different from those businesses we will look at today. Being able to compare and contrast those features or differences will be important when answering questions in your exam.

  • Tell students: Sole traders are small businesses. When a business needs to raise finance to grow it usually means the business will change its legal structure, either to take on partners or to incorporate and become a company. A sole trader does not have a legally separate identity to the business and so they have unlimited liability. It is simple and inexpensive to set up as a sole trader, and an advantage of being a small business is that personal service can be offered to customers.

The features of a private limited company

  • Tell students: Our starter exercise recapped the key features of unincorporated companies which have unlimited liability.
  • Tell students: Today we will start to look at incorporated businesses: that is companies which have a separate legal identity from that of their owners. Because incorporated companies are seen as distinct from their owners, those owners are not liable for the debts of the company.
  • Ask a volunteer to suggest an answer to the question: What would we call this situation where the owners are not liable to repay the debts of a business if it becomes bankrupt? (Answer: Limited liability)
  • Tell students: Limited liability means that the responsibility of the owners to repay any debts the business owes is limited to just the money that an owner has already invested in the company. This means that the owners would not be forced to pay money from their own bank accounts or to sell their personal possessions such as cars or houses if their company goes out of business or if they owe money to other companies or individuals.
  • Display the Slideshow: The features of a private limited company and click on Slide 1. Tell students to make a note of the information in their exercise books.

  • Tell students: Although Dyson is a very large business that sells in many countries around the world, most limited companies are small businesses. However, a few large companies do choose to remain as private limited companies, such as LEGO in Denmark and Mars in the USA.

  • Tell students: There are some more important features of a limited company which are different to a sole trader. These are that they can sell shares, which is an important way of raising finance and may allow the company to grow more easily. This finance is sometimes called ‘capital’ for the business. The shareholders of private limited companies also elect one or more directors to run the business on their behalf, but in smaller companies they might carry out this role themselves.
  • Tell students: Because an incorporated business has a separate legal identity to the owners, the business itself pays tax on its profits. This is called corporation tax. In an unincorporated business there is no distinction between the company and the owner, so sole traders and partners pay personal tax on the profits they make from their business. This is called income tax.
  • Tell students: One of the reasons that some sole traders choose that form of ownership is because it is the simplest form of business to set up. Becoming incorporated as a ‘proper’ company with a separate legal identity to the owners is more complicated and involves completing two documents, sending them to the government and paying a small fee.

  • Tell students: VAT registration (in the UK) is not mandatory for private limited companies and many small ones will not need to register if they do not earn enough revenue.

Task 1: The sales pitch (7 minutes)

  • Tell students that this activity involves working as the business they ‘set up’ in the last lesson so they should move to sit either on their own if they were sole traders or with the other students who are partners in their partnership.
  • Display the Slideshow:Task 1 and go through the information with students. Explain that the first two to three bullet points are part of the information they produced at the end of the last lesson (Lesson5).

LINK

  • Tell students: This ‘investment’ your business seeks will be the money that will help the business set up, grow or outcompete its rivals.
  • Explain that, after this task, each student will have an opportunity to buy some shares in one or more other businesses that another student or group of students are starting.
  • Tell students that they will each have £20,000 to invest. Multiply this sum by the number of students in the class and confirm to students the total investment capital available (for example, if there are 30 students in the class, there will be £600,000 in total for all the companies that are seeking investment; there may be roughly 15 companies depending on how many are sole traders or partnerships).
  • Give each ‘business’ three sticky notes and tell them they have 6 minutes to complete the first part of the activity. Tell students: When you have decided on your ‘sales pitch’ you need to put those details onto your three sticky notes. You will not be given any more so make sure you get it right first time! You will need to be very concise in what you write.
  • One minute before the students’ 6-minute time-limit is up, warn them that any company that has not put its ‘sales pitch’ onto the board will miss the opportunity to seek finance and investors.
  • After 6 minutes, tell students to come up and stick their sticky notes onto the board to ‘advertise’ to the investors.
  • You may wish to take a photograph of the sticky notes students have posted on the board in case students need to refer back to their businesses in a future lesson (Lesson 14).

Task 2: The elevator pitch (10 minutes)

  • You may wish to give students more time to complete Task 1 and they will potentially have a lot of pitches to read in this follow-up task, Task 2. There are two options:
    • Option 1: Run the task as described below, with students working at speed and to a tight focus.
    • Option 2: Allow students more time to read the pitches on the board. This likely to mean that you will need to run this lesson over two sessions.
  • Tell students: You are now potential investors, rather than working in your business. Come up to the board in small groups to decide if you want to invest your £20,000 into any of the companies.
  • Display the Slideshow:Task 2 and go through the information with students.

LINK

  • Tell students that you will choose who comes up and in what order, but that no-one is allowed to touch the board and if they do so they will be barred from investing. Explain that they will only have 60 seconds (the time it takes an elevator to reach the top floor of a very tall tower block) to make their decisions so they will probably only have time to quickly scan the pitches. When students have made their decision, they should note down the name/s of the company/companies and how much they want to invest.
  • Tell students: You may want to split your £20,000 between multiple companies. This might mean thosecompanies will seek other shareholders who will provide them with larger amounts of money.
  • You should know from the sales pitches how much each business is seeking in investment. If this is not clear, check with any of those missing. Ask the four students whose companies are seeking the least amount of investment to come up to the board first.
  • After 60 seconds, bring the next group of students up to the board and continue to rotate groups every 60 seconds.
  • Remind students: While you wait for your turn to come up to the board, you should be writing questions in your exercise books that you would want to ask the owners of the companies you are interested in investing in to help you make decisions.
  • Remind them: Once you have selected companies you are interested in investing in, you should update your questions based on the specific information you know about the companies.

Support

For students who struggle with The features of a private limited company:

  • In Teaching session 1, some students may struggle with understanding the difference between a private limited company and a partnership. Explain that a ltd is an arrangement that provides more protection to its owners, but that, in return, those owners need to be bound by more rules about reporting results, sharing information with rivals and the public and have to pay tax in a different way. You could also refer students to Table 4.1 on p. 29 of the Student Book showing advantages and disadvantages of a private limited company.
  • In Task 1 of Student work 1, some students may struggle with coming up with a ‘pitch’. Suggest they think about the opportunities their business presents for someone else by thinking about what attracted them to start that sort of company in the first place.
  • In Task 2 of Student work 1, some students may struggle with coming up with questions. Provide a prompt by explaining that they will need to think about what happens if there are disputes, how profits and responsibilities will be divided etc. There are also prompts in the Student Book: p. 29 in the bullet points where the Memorandum of Association and Articles of Association are explained.

Advantages and disadvantages of private limited companies

  • Tell students: When deciding whether they want to take in new owners (shareholders) to a sole trader/partnership the current owner(s) will need to think about the good and bad points related to becoming private limited companies.
  • Ask a volunteer(s) to suggest an advantage of becoming a private limited company (compared to being a sole trader).
  • Click on Slide 1 of the slideshow to reveal the answers.

LINK

  • Tell students to make a note of the advantages in their exercise books. Explain bullets two and three in more detail:
    • Shareholders can decide if they want to look for new owners to help run and expand the business and to potentially bring in new investment. Most private limited companies will share profits between the shareholders in the same ratio that they each invested money into the company. This means that if one person initially put in £100,000 and later, two other shareholders joined and each contributed £50,000 then the first shareholder would usually get 50% of all of the profits and the other two would each get 25%. It may also be agreed that the 50% shareholder gets two votes on major decisions while the other shareholders only get one each but this is up to the owners to decide. Explain that this is not always the case (and would usually be set out in the Articles of Association) because sometimes, if a shareholder is providing more expertise, has contributed some of their personal assets such as a car or machinery, they may be given more shares than another owner.
    • If ownership is not shared equally, companies usually create a larger number of shares to reflect the greater contribution that one shareholder might be making and the profits would usually be split, based on the proportion of the total amount of shares that each person holds.
    • Tell students: For example, I might want to set up a private limited company that offers motivational speaking. If I asked a well-known sportsperson to be a shareholder in the business they might not contribute any money to set up the business, leaving me to inject all of the money needed. I might also work full-time in the business, while they would just spend an hour or two every week delivering the motivational speeches but we might share an equal shareholding and share profits equally.
    • Tell them: This might seem an unusual or unfair balance but I might be very happy with this arrangement because the sportsperson is contributing their celebrity status and that would attract far more business to the company than if I ran it on my own, hence it would be a good arrangement for both shareholders.
    • Tell them: We would also enjoy the benefit of being able to decide who we did or did not want to admit into the company as new shareholders. For example, we might also decide to offer a share to another well-known person because of the interest they would create in our business. However, if someone unknown asked to join the business we could reject that request if we wanted to, even if they were willing to pay a vast amount of money.
    • Tell them: Therefore, the shareholders get to retain full control of their business.
  • Give students 1 minute to think of any disadvantages related to private limited company status and note them down. They should work individually.
  • If they need support, suggest that the ‘elevator pitch’ activity (Student work 1) might give them some ideas or they could think about some of the advantages of being an unincorporated business and whether being a ltd might mean those benefits are lost and so become ‘disadvantages’ of this form of ownership.
  • Ask a volunteer for an idea about disadvantages. (Answers below)
  • Click on Slide 2 to reveal the answers.
  • Explain the disadvantages while students make a note of them:
    • In most countries, there is a fee which must be paid to the government in order to establish a private limited company.
    • If owners decide to bring in new shareholders, they may receive investment but will have to share the profits the company makes with more people and they will also have to share decision-making responsibility with those people.
    • This can be good and bad as it helps to spread workload and responsibility but can lead to disputes. Therefore, setting out how to manage the company via the Memorandum and Articles of Association is crucial.
    • It is not necessarily easy to find people who want to become shareholders as they will usually have to contribute money and take some role in running the company. (Lesson 7 looks at how some larger companies can solve this problem)
    • Private limited companies have to file certain basic information with the government which is then made publicly available so competitors will get to find out some information about a ltd company which they may find useful.

Answers

  1. Two key sets of documents are needed before a business can trade as a limited company. These include the memorandum of association and the articles of association.
  2. Khan Sugar Mills Ltd is a private limited company. It is a family business and is owned by three brothers, a venture capitalist called FarmVent and some of the senior managers. Three quarters of the company is owned by the Khan brothers – Abdul, Mohsin and Nabil; they each own 25 per cent of the shares. FarmVent own 20 per cent of the company while senior managers have a small 5 per cent interest. Since the three brothers own three-quarters of the company, together they have complete control over the company.
  3. Private limited companies, such as Khan Sugar Mills Ltd, enjoy a number of advantages. One significant advantage is that the company cannot be taken over by outsiders. This is because the shares are not on open sale. Shares in private limited companies cannot be transferred without the permission of the other shareholders. They cannot be bought on the stock market. This is because only shares in PLCs can be traded on the stock market. Therefore, Khan Sugar Mills Ltd is safe from an unwanted takeover. Another advantage is that all of the shareholders have limited liability. This means that they can only lose the amount of money they originally invested in the company if it were to collapse. Khan Sugar Mills Ltd’s shareholders cannot be made to use personal wealth to pay off business debts. Limited liability means they enjoy legal protection from business creditors.

Disadvantages:

It may be difficult to raise the amount of money needed to expand the company beyond its one factory in Multan if the brothers decide they wish to do so. This is because ltds can only access share capital from a small pool of owners, unlike plcs who can sell additional shares on the stock market to raise large amounts of money to fund expansion.

There is potential for disputes among the owners because some senior managers have a shareholding in the company and so if the family (or FarmVent) wish to cut costs by reducing the number of managers or staff, they may not be able to achieve this if the managers who own shares do not agree to the changes.
