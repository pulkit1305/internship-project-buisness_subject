Different forms of social enterprise

  • Tell students that you are going to ask them a question, and when suggesting an answer they should focus on the businesses, rather than their owners.
  • Ask students: Who can suggest a non-financial objective that some private sector businesses may want to achieve? (Answer: Social objectives)
  • Ask students: Who can remember what we meant by social objectives (previously taught in Lesson 2)? Can you give me some examples?
  • Possible answers:
    1. Supporting certain groups of the population (old people, disadvantaged children)
    2. Protecting animals or wildlife
    3. Restoring and protecting the natural environment
    4. Improving the services on offer to a community.
  • Tell students: Although many companies might try to achieve these objectives while also seeking to make profits, some organisations focus on achieving these types of objective as their primary reason for existence. Examples include Oxfam, which focuses on eliminating poverty in countries across the world, Shonaquip, which helps promote the inclusion of families with disabled children into their communities in South Africa and St Pauli, which is a sports club in Hamburg, Germany that is owned by its 30,000 members and has five guiding principles to set out how it will support its members and local community.
  • Tell students: These businesses are known as social enterprises.
  • Click on Slide 1 of the slideshow and tell students to copy the information about social enterprises into their exercise books.
  • Click on Slide 2 and click to reveal the first bullet.
  • Tell students:
    1. A common form of social enterprise is a charity. Many charities exist to raise money for, or awareness of, a specific good cause within society. This could be protecting a local green space or seeking a cure for malaria. Charities raise money by inviting donations from members of the public, other businesses or governments and then, once they have paid their operating costs, spend the surplus on supporting the specific cause they raised the money for.
    2. Charities raise money in different ways. Some seek to promote the importance of their cause and so provide a strong argument for why members of the public should donate to support their activities. Examples include the World Widelife Fund (set up to protect nature) and the Red Cross Society of China (founded as a humanitarian organisation). Others sell certain goods or services and so generate a profit from this part of their operations which is used to fund the social campaigns that are the ‘mission’ of the company. For example, Yayasan Solemen in Indonesia runs a ‘smile shop’ selling donated new or pre-loved goods to raise funds for poor households where family members have disabilities.
  • Click to reveal the second bullet on Slide 2.
  • Tell students: Another form of social enterprise is the cooperative. Cooperatives are formed to support the needs of a particular group, such as customers or workers.
  • Click to reveal the third bullet on Slide 2.
  • Tell students: In the UK, the Cooperative Group (Co-op) is owned by its members who each pay £1 to join. This makes those members the ‘owners’ of the cooperative, which provides groceries, funeral, banking and insurance services. Members receive discounts on Co-op’s products and services. They also share decision-making and the profits are re-distributed to those members who can then spend their share in the company’s shops. This is an example of a customer, or consumer cooperative.
  • Click to reveal the fourth bullet on Slide 2.
  • Tell students: There are also retail cooperatives, which are when groups of independently owned shops work together to source the goods that they sell. In this way they can benefit from economies of scale by purchasing stock in much larger quantity than the individual member shops could do if they bought it independently. This allows them to compete with larger companies on the basis of cost. In the USA, ShopRite is a retail cooperative brand under which 50 different private businesses work together to buy groceries for the 300 or more stores that operate under the ShopRite name, which consumers come to trust due to the fact it is seen as a single larger business by most consumers.
  • Click to reveal the final bullet on Slide 2.
  • Tell students: Another form of cooperative is a worker cooperative. In India the Urulungal Labour Co-operative is owned and managed by workers who created the business to build important infrastructure, such as transport systems, housing and agriculture. This provides paid work and guarantees reasonable working conditions and a say in the projects workers carry out. It also ensures that important projects get built because they are the ones most needed by the community.
  • Click on Slide 3.
  • Give students 3 minutes to match the features to the forms of social enterprise. They should work individually.
  • Go through the features one-at-a-time, asking students to show answers on a mini whiteboard before clicking to reveal the correct answer.
  • Click on Slide 4.
  • Tell students: The word stakeholder refers to any party that is affected by the operations of a business or organisation. We learned about shareholders in Lesson 6. They are people who own a portion or a ‘share’ of a company
  • Give students 2 minutes to identify the key features of a social enterprise (of any kind). They should work in pairs.
  • Warn students that they need to read the features carefully. Some relate to private companies and not to social enterprises so they should not list those.
  • Ask a student to suggest an answer and continue until all features have been identified. As you do so, discuss any misconceptions. This may involve clarifying the following points:
    1. Accountable and transparent: a private company will usually seek to keep certain information private rather than allow rivals to understand how it runs. A social enterprise is likely to want to promote the way it is run and the basis for decision-making in order to justify what it does to its members, donors or the wider public.
    2. Income generation: social enterprises rely on donations or income from selling goods and services, while private companies can raise large amounts of money by selling shares etc.
    3. Objectives: the main goal of private businesses is presumed to be making a profit for the owners (or possibly growing the size of the business to increase its value so it can be sold at a profit).
  • Click on Slide 5 to confirm the correct answers.

  • After 7 minutes, tell students to check their answers to the first five questions. They should then read their answer to Q6 to their partner who must try to guess the social enterprise being described. Students should swap so each tries to guess the others’ social enterprise.

Support

For students who struggle with Different forms of social enterprise:

  • In Teaching session 1, some students may struggle to identify features specific to a social enterprise on Slide 4 of the slideshow. Suggest that they focus on what is supposed to be the primary goal of private business – to create the maximum financial return for the owners – and then avoid those features as they do not generally apply to social enterprises.
  • In Student work 1, on Question 6 of Worksheet 1, some students may struggle to identify a specific social enterprise. Suggest they think about a charity that they know and then describe the type of person or thing that charity supports and how it does so.

Arguments for social enterprises

  • Tell students: We have already seen that a stakeholder is any individual or group with an interest in the operation of a business.
  • Ask: Can anyone suggest who the stakeholder in our school might be? 
  • Give students 1 minute to discuss with a partner and ask for volunteers to give their answers.
  • Possible answers:
    1. The students, who are the ‘customers’ that directly receive the service provided
    2. Families who benefit in different ways from a student’s education
    3. The local community who may be affected by students making a positive contribution, but also negatively by crowding on the roads at the start and end of the school day, or litter and noise if students or teachers are not good ‘community citizens’
    4. The wider society which benefits from having students who are educated in a responsible manner and learn how to make positive contributions
    5. Companies who do not have to pay to train workers in the skills they are taught by a school
    6. Suppliers who sell textbooks to the school and its students
    7. Other schools who may have to ‘compete’ with our school to attract students but may benefit from having healthy competition in terms of opponents to play sports against.
  • Mention any of these groups if they do not come up in student answers.
  • Explain to students that social enterprises offer some benefits to stakeholders beyond those that they might have been set up with the primary objective of supporting. This can often mean that social enterprises gain an advantage over other ‘for-profit’ companies. 
  • Tell students:
    1. For example, One Water is a company that provides bottled water and other products. It has an objective of raising £50m by 2030 to help ensure that everyone around the world has access to clean drinking water. As well as direct beneficiaries who have wells created, the company also creates economic benefits in those countries by employing local people and educating communities. 
    2. One Water also operates on a carbon-neutral basis and holds the highest level of global certification for ‘community’: legally assuring that it assesses the impact of its decisions on workers, customers, community and the wider environment.
  • Ask students: How do you think that One Water’s commitment to ‘community’ might allow it to increase the sales of its products? 
  • Possible answers:
    1. Customers are more likely to buy from companies that they believe are trying to support certain stakeholders other than just their shareholders. 
    2. Workers may be more committed to a company that they feel supports them and their interests and this will improve motivation and the impact workers have on their company.
    3. The company may be able to charge a higher price because consumers believe it is worth paying extra to buy from a company that they believe takes an ethical stance on issues.
    4. Charities often pay lower or no tax and so can offer lower prices and more support for their intended causes.
    5. A ‘social objective’ behind a company may create positive stories in the press and lead customers to recommend the company to friends, improving its public image and marketing it more effectively than its ‘for-profit’ rivals.
  • Tell students that as you work through the points, they should make notes in their exercise books. They should copy the text on the slide and note down the additional information you give them so that they understand and remember it when they come to revise from their notes. Tell them they should keep their notes concise.
  • Click to reveal the first bullet.
  • Tell students: Some customers will deliberately seek out and buy from businesses whose values they support, while others may factor the ‘social benefit’ into decisions about whom to buy from.
  • Click to reveal the second bullet.
  • Tell students: Not only will workers increase their efforts when working for a company they believe in and that focuses on their interests, but this may also attract customers who value this approach to a wider range of stakeholders, other than just the owners.
  • Click to reveal the third bullet.
  • Tell students: Being able to benefit from the profits made by a business may attract more members and customers. Similarly, a ‘say’ in decisions as a member may also attract some people who want to effect social change.
  • Click to reveal the final bullet.
  • Tell students: Many social enterprises are small and local and so may have an increased connection to their community and customers. Knowing members of staff and the owners may create more of a bond with customers, meaning they return more often or spend more money.
  • Explain to students that the benefits listed on the slide may not occur for all social enterprises but can be powerful contributors to the success of some organisations. Additionally, there are benefits that are more difficult to assess or describe but are connected to a general sense of support for businesses that are ‘trying to make a difference’ in some way.
  • Tell students that they have learned about the different ways in which organisations are owned and operated in Lessons 5‒10 and are going to do an activity that allows them to put what they have learned into practice.

  • Hand out Worksheet 2.
  • Explain that this is a whole-class activity and that they need to work together to agree how they will structure their society on a new planet, Terra 2.
  • Tell students: The reason that most societies have a government is to ensure that society and the businesses and institutions which make it up function effectively.
  • This exercise will be judged by how well students achieve the various tasks they are given within the exercise. It is up to you how you choose to structure the government to run your society but I would suggest having one leader who will be responsible for the class, ultimately ensuring it meets all of the requirements and helps to support decision-making. This leader would probably also be supported in government by some ministers who might be responsible for helping the groups that might be chosen to focus on different aspects of Terra 2’s activities. Examples could be as follows but it is up to you to decide how to divide up the responsibilities:
    1. Minister for public services
    2. Minister for infrastructure
    3. Minister for transport
    4. Minister for private businesses
    5. Minister for citizens’ rights.
  • Tell students: The skills that these members of government have will be important in helping you decide whom to select. Think about:
    1. having the ability to manage groups of people with different ideas and opinions, and to help them make good decisions
    2. time-management skills to keep the group on course to deliver what is required within the timescales
    3. being able to take difficult decisions if asked to provide a final decision based on evidence they hear
    4. being good at dealing with other citizens in a friendly and supportive but authoritative way
    5. being capable of winning the respect of their fellow citizens.
  • Tell students: The main objective of this activity will be to decide how to provide the goods and services Terra 2 will depend on.

Task 1: Forming a government (4 minutes)

  • Tell students that the first task involves Questions 1 and 2 and that they have 4 minutes to complete this task.
  • After 3 minutes, warn students that they are going to need to tell you who will form the government of the society in just 1 minute’s time.
  • At the end of this time, inform the government that they are to come up to the front of the classroom and run the remainder of the activity.
  • The government should sit on a panel at the front of the class and lead the discussion from this point onwards.

Task 2: Planning Terra 2’s model society (18 minutes)

  • Allow this activity to be student-led as far as possible. However, do steer students and remind them that their key objectives from the activity are:
    1. To establish the essential services that the society is going to require as it grows from 240 to 2,000 people.
    2. To focus on the best way to provide these services (considering whether public ownership, private companies or social enterprises will be the best way of delivering and managing these services).
  • Tell students that it is up to them how they carry out the activity but achieving the goals is crucial. They should also ensure they establish an inclusive society where the views of all citizens are valued and their participation is encouraged.
  • Tell students they have 18 minutes to come up with notes for a proposal for how to organise the society and will need to choose someone/some people who will (briefly) present their decisions after that time. Students will present their proposal in the End-of-lesson activity.
  • If students are not coming up with a sufficiently broad range of business and other services, use the list in the answers below to guide them.

Answers

  1. Social enterprises aim to improve human and environmental well-being rather than make a profit for owners. Generally, social enterprises have a clear social and/or environmental mission. They generate most of their income through trade or donations and reinvest most of their profits. They are majority controlled in the interests of the social mission and are accountable and transparent. In this case, Save the Children aims to improve the well-being of children across the world. In particular, Save the Children raises money to run world-class programmes to save children’s lives and challenge world leaders to keep to their promises to give children a brighter future.
  2. Most charities rely on gifts and donations to fund their social activities. In this case Save the Children raises very large amounts of money globally. In 2015, it generated around US$2100 million. According to the pie chart in Figure 3.2 a large proportion of this money came from institutions and governments – 58 per cent. The next largest source was from individual donations – 25 per cent. Individuals are often generous when giving to causes that protect the interests of children. Corporations and foundations are another important source. This might be companies and special charitable foundations. Save the Children is a large international charity. Smaller charities might organise fund-raising events such as cake sales, sponsored activities and selling greetings cards. Some also run business ventures, such as charity shops.

Encourage them to summarise some of the alternatives for the ownership or delivery of key services that were not chosen and the reasons behind the decisions taken.

Alternative activities

  • These resources can be used if you are splitting the content of this lesson into two sessions.

  • Tell students: During the last lesson (Lesson 9) and as part of your homework, you had to assess the case for privatising the Australian Broadcasting Corporation. We are now going to vote to see which group presented the most convincing arguments.
  • Tell students to raise their hands if they support the privatisation of the ABC. Count the votes.
  • Establish whether there is majority support for privatisation.
  • Display Slideshow: ABC: Privatisation or nationalisation? to remind students of some of the potential problems created by their choice.
  • Give students 2 minutes to discuss some potential solutions to the problems with a partner.
  • Ask for students’ suggestions about how to resolve some of these issues.
  • Possible answers:
    1. A government body or independent regulator sets the maximum prices which private sector companies can charge
    2. Governments or charities could provide funding to allow low-income consumers to continue consuming important goods
    3. The government may sell off some profitable parts of ABC to generate cash to allow it to continue providing loss-making public service programmes
    4. Set up an independent body to set and enforce rules that ensure all programmes are politically neutral.
