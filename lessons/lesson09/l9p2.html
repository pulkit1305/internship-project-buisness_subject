Presenting students’ franchising research

  • Choose a student to present information about one of the franchise opportunities that they researched as part of their homework from the last lesson (Lesson 8). Tell the student they have 1 minute to deliver all of the information but not to say whether they would take out the franchise or not.
  • Tell the rest of the class: Listen carefully to the information that is being presented so that you are able to ask questions relating to whether you think buying that franchise is a good idea or not.
  • Following the presentation, invite volunteers to ask questions about the franchise opportunity. Spend 2 minutes on the questioning.
  • Sensible questions could include:
    • Why did you choose that sector/specific business?
    • How much are you likely to earn as a franchisee in the first year?
    • How much will the total costs be during the first year?
    • How much additional investment will be needed and where will the student get that money from?
    • Are any benefits (or costs) offered by that particular franchisor that we did not discuss during the last lesson?
  • If no students have asked the last question, ask it yourself.
  • Possible answers to benefits/costs question:
    • Benefits – a dedicated ‘launch consultant’ who will work out the best ways to plan the opening and initial operation. A property agent who will work with me to find the most suitable premises for my franchise.
    • Costs – I have to buy uniforms from the franchisor, I have to take part in certain nationally advertised promotions whether I want to or not
  • Tell students: Discuss in pairs for 1 minute to remind yourselves of the advantages and disadvantages of franchises.
  • Ask for volunteers to share advantages or disadvantages.

The objectives of public corporations

  • Tell students: During the course so far we have looked at businesses which have one key objective in common: they will all be trying to make a profit for the owners. Today, we are going to investigate a type of company that exists for a different reason: to provide a service to members of the public.
  • Ask students: What are some examples of companies that exist to provide a service to members of the public?
  • Possible answers:
    • police force
    • armed forces
    • schools
    • hospitals
    • utility providers (water, electricity etc. providers)
  • Tell students: in order to focus on providing a service to the community, these businesses are not owned by private individuals who want to make money from owning them. Instead, they tend to be state-owned. This means they are owned by the government and operated on behalf of citizens. These companies are called public corporations.
  • Tell them: Public corporations are incorporated businesses so they are officially separate legal entities from the people in government who run them. They are usually funded by the state or government and their managers are accountable to specific ministers within the government.
  • Tell them: In some countries, some public corporations may be run in order to make a profit, but this is not usually the case. Examples of public corporations operated in order to make a profit for their government, and therefore its citizens, are Emirates airlines, owned by the government of Dubai, and Norges Bank IM, owned by the Norwegian government. Both seek to make a profit in order to bring money in to the country for the government to spend on providing services for the citizens.
  • Tell them: Public corporations are often part of the public sector. We learned about this in Lesson 2.
  • Ask: Can anyone tell me what the public sector is? (Answer: The part of the economy where businesses are operated by local or national governments.)
  • Tell students: Businesses owned by private individuals are in the private sector.

Often a public corporation that tries to make a profit may also have other aims, such as a state-owned airline that wants to bring more visitors to a country, elevate the country’s public image or publicise the country.
    • ‘Funded by the government’ – although some governments may make contributions to the running of private sector businesses (for example, paying farmers to ensure there is enough food or providing funding to charities), public corporations often receive all of their funding from the government (e.g. state-run schools or the police force). General taxation is used to provide that funding.

Answers

<source info> page 409 Page 3 – Edexcel International GCSE (9–1) Business Student Book: Teacher Resource Pack - ActiveTeach (activeteachonline.com)

  1. The Ugandan National and Water Sewerage Corporation is owned by the Ugandan government and run by a board of directors, which is accountable to a government minister.
  2. The main objective of NWSC is to provide a public service; it has committed to the provision of good quality customer service. NWSC aims to supply everyone with clean safe water within 200 metres of all towns and urban centres (coverage is currently 78 per cent). On the NWSC website, it states that ‘The Customer is the reason we exist. We do everything to the delight of our customer.’
  3. In the private sector, most businesses aim to make a profit. However, organisations in the public sector aim to provide a public service. Sometimes providing a public service may be unprofitable, therefore, private sector firms would not be interested in supplying the market. Consequently the government needs to run and own organisations so that the provision of particular public services can be funded from taxpayers’ money.

Also, some industries, like water supply, are crucial to the well-being or security of the nation. Therefore, since they are so important, it is arguably better if the government takes control so that supply and quality can be guaranteed.

Arguments for and against public ownership

  • Choose an organisation in your country that you know is a public corporation for the initial discussion in this teaching session. An ideal option would be the police force, which is used here as an example, but an alternative could be emergency medical services or providing street lighting.
  • Ask students: Why don’t your families have to pay directly to benefit from having a police force to keep you safe and maintain law and order?
  • Give students 1 minute to think about this and note down ideas in their exercise books. They should work individually.
  • Share ideas as a class.
  • Possible answers:
    • Because that is what we pay taxes for
    • Because I/my family would not choose to pay
    • We might not be able to afford it
  • Tell students: Most people generally agree that it is necessary to have a police force, but there is much less agreement about how much they would be willing to pay, exactly what that police force should do, how they should do it or how the police should be paid. Some people would also not be able to pay anything because they would not be able to afford it.
  • Tell them: For some goods this would not be a problem ‒ for example, if you cannot afford a car or a foreign holiday, most societies accept you would not receive that good because a business needs to be able to charge you for it. This underpins most business transactions: that businesses exist to make a profit by providing the goods that consumers want to buy.
  • Tell them: However, for certain goods, we would want them to be available to everyone ‒ not just for those who can afford to buy them. Police protection is one example of this.
  • Ask students: How many other goods or services can you think of that fall into this category?
  • Invite students to share ideas as a class and make a list on the board.
  • Possible answers:
    • Education
    • Some areas of healthcare
    • Parks, beaches and open spaces
    • Roads, bridges etc.
  • Tell students: Most countries provide some level of education for all children for free because there are a lot of benefits to the individual and society of being educated. Therefore, countries do not want those who cannot afford to pay for an education to miss out on those benefits. So having a public corporation to provide services such as education, street lighting or basic healthcare is deemed necessary to make society function and to improve the lives of the poorest people who would not be able to afford those goods or services themselves.

Therefore, just one company tends to supply water to a city, because they can dig just one set of pipes. This means the water supply industry is a natural monopoly because the most cost efficient way of supplying that product is to only have one company do so.   • Share ideas as a class. Ask volunteers to describe any reasons that public ownership may not be beneficial.
  • Possible answers:
    • Cost
    • Political interference
    • Inefficiency
  • Tell students: A key argument against public ownership is that it costs a lot of money to provide many services (for example health or education services) and so the costs have to be paid through taxes. Also, if companies are to be nationalised, there is a large one-off cost. In the article, Forgemasters ‘only’ cost a relatively low £2.6m but needs hundreds of millions of pounds of further investment, which taxpayers will have to provide.
  • Tell them: Another argument against public ownership is that the lack of a motive to make profits in the public sector can make companies inefficient. For example, would you be more careful about turning off lights at home or would you walk instead of using a car if you were paying for the electricity or petrol? In public sector companies, any losses resulting from a lack of control over spending and costs are paid by the government. In a private sector company, losses have to be covered by that company or they risk going out of business. So, in the public sector there may not be the same incentive to reduce costs or be efficient than in a private sector company.
  • Tell them: While there may be a strong argument for politicians to control companies in certain industries, not everyone agrees which businesses should be controlled by governments and which by private companies. Some people argue that too many industries are influenced by political reasons when companies are public corporations.

Task 2: Preparing the privatisation debate (7 minutes)

  • Tell students: You are going to work in groups to prepare for a class debate on the future of the ABC. We will hold the debate at the end of the lesson (in the End-of-lesson activity).
  • In Task 1, the students chose to be either privatisers or nationalisers. Split these groups down further so that you have four equal-size groups of:
    • Privatisation – proposers
    • Privatisation – defenders
    • Nationalisation – proposers
    • Nationalisation – defenders
  • The ‘proposers’ will prepare an argument for either nationalisation or privatisation. The ‘defenders’ need to predict the arguments the other side will use and prepare a counter-argument against them.
  • Ensure that each group is of mixed-ability to support students who may not be as confident in preparing and prioritising arguments.
  • Tell these groups to space themselves around the classroom. Students will need to take exercise books with them, so that they can refer to their answers to Task 1.
  • Ensure each group understands what is expected of them, then give them 5 minutes to prepare.
  • During Task 2, monitor ‘proposer’ groups to make sure they have been able to prepare three coherent points/reasons.

Answers

<source info> page 410 Page 3 – Edexcel International GCSE (9–1) Business Student Book: Teacher Resource Pack - ActiveTeach (activeteachonline.com)

  1. Privatisation is the process of transferring public sector resources, such as public corporations, to the private sector. In this case, a number of airports and airport facilities have been privatised. For example, in 2013, the Brazilian Government raised US$9100 million by privatising two major airports ahead of the 2014 World Cup.
  2. Governments have historically preferred to keep control of businesses operating airports. Even as recently as 2006, only about 2 per cent of the world’s airports were owned or managed by the private sector. The reason for this is because airports are considered as strategically important to the nation. If the provision of a particular service is vital to the well-being or security of the nation it is considered desirable for the government to maintain control so that continuity of supply and quality can be guaranteed.
  3. One motive for privatisation is to raise money. If the government sells a public corporation, such as an airport, the money can be used by the government. In this case for example, the Brazilian government plans to sell more airports to raise money to help reduce its budget deficit. Another possible motive for privatisation is that airports are likely to be run more efficiently in the private sector. According to Ahmed Bukalla, director of operations at the Department of Civil Aviation, Sharjah International Airport, UAE. ‘When privatisation comes, there will be pressure for further productivity gains, and airport operators will need to be even more innovative and adopt new technologies to stay competitive.’ Therefore in the private sector businesses will serve the public better because if they don't they risk ‘going bust’.
  • Hold a class debate to round off the work students completed in Student work 2. Students will need to be in the same groups as they worked in for Student work 2, but just in the two ‘privatiser’ and nationaliser’ groups.
  • Tell students: We are going to debate whether the Australian Broadcasting Corporation should remain a public corporation or be privatised.
  • Explain to students that the homework will rely on the arguments they have used as part of the debate and that they will vote on the question during the next lesson.
  • Give groups 1 minute to decide who is going to present each of their arguments and who is going to respond to each argument.
  • Tell students: We will first hear an argument for its privatisation. Who is going to put forward that argument?
  • Allow a student to propose one reason for privatisation and then tell the ‘nationaliser’ group to respond to the argument.
  • Allow a student to propose one reason for nationalisation and then tell the ‘privatiser’ group to respond to the argument.
  • Repeat with further arguments and counter-arguments until the end of the lesson.
  • Students should answer Chapter Q4 about the ABC case study on p. 43 of the Student Book.
  • When assigning the homework, tell students: Ensure you provide a good, detailed coverage of at least three reasons for privatisation and three counter-arguments. You will also need to provide an overall recommendation for or against privatisation that uses evidence from the case study to support it.
  • Q4 requires a detailed analysis of both sides of the argument for privatisation. Students will be required to revisit the pros/cons they learned during the lesson and then to apply these to the specific requirements of ABC, demonstrating skills of application. They will use this context to develop a detailed response and so practise those important writing skills.
