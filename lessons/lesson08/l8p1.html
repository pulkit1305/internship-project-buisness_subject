Link

In this lesson, students will learn to:

  • understand the main features of a franchised business
  • examine the advantages and disadvantages of operating a franchise.

Students should already be familiar with:

  • large public limited companies (see Lesson 7)
  • economies of scale (seeLesson 3).

Although students have not encountered the franchise model before, they may be familiar with many businesses that use franchising.

  • franchise: an arrangement where a business (the franchisor) allows another (the franchisee) to trade under their name and use their business systems
  • franchise fee: the cost of buying a franchise
  • franchisee: a business that buys the right to trade under the name of another business and to use the business system of that other business
  • franchisor: a business that sells the right to another business to trade under their name and to use their business system
  • multinational company: a large business with significant service or production operations in at least two countries
  • royalty payments: ongoing payments from the franchisee.

Pearson Edexcel International GCSE Business (9–1) Student Book: p. 23 and pp. 31–32


No equipment is needed for this lesson.

  • Some students may think that a franchise is a legal structure or specific form of ownership, which it is not. Explain that franchisors will usually be limited companies and mightinsist that their franchisees adopt this ownership structure too. Stress that franchising is a business method rather than a specific legal structure.
  • Some students will get confused between the roles of a franchisee and a franchisor because the names are so similar. Explain that the franchisoris the biggercompany and will run (with an ‘r’) the relationship between the two businesses.
