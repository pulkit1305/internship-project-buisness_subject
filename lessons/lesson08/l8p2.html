Introduction to franchising

  • Tell students: Today we will be looking at how some very large businesses operate and an important way in which they interact with much smaller businesses.
  • Put students in pairs and give them 1 minute to discuss the names of the biggest fast food chains that they are aware of.
  • Share findings as a class.
  • Possible answers:
    • McDonald's
    • KFC
    • Burger King
    • Pizza Express
    • Subway
    • Pizza Hut.
  • Tell students: Mostlarge fast food chains offer franchises.In 2022, Subway had nearly 40,000 shops worldwide,and the company is less than 50 years old. Similarly, McDonald's is the largest chain of fast food shops and is also less than 80 years old. Both of these huge chains used a system called franchising to allow them to grow to the size they are today. KFC and Burger King use this system too.
  • Tell students: Franchising is a system where a large, established business allows a small private business to use its brand name and products to operate a new outlet of that business on behalf of the big business (the franchisor). In return for being allowed to benefit from the head-start this gives small businesses, they pay the franchisor for the right to use the name. This franchise fee is called a royalty payment.

  • Tell students: Franchising itself is not a specific form of ownership/legal structure for a company. Most franchisors are public limited companies, and franchisees are small businesses and will usually be sole traders or private limited companies. Therefore, franchising is a method of operating a business that may suit certain large companies and also entrepreneurs.
  • Tell students: Many large businesses choose to operate a franchise model because it allows them to grow quickly.
  • Explain: This is because they can use enthusiastic entrepreneurs who are looking to set up their own businesses that they can operate and run, but those people mightnot have a specific business idea or feel they cannot compete with a large chain (such as the franchisor)so will choose to operate one of those chain outlets rather than compete against it.
  • Tell students: Another reason is that franchising does not require the franchisor to provide as much capital as if the business were opening new branches itself. It relies on the franchisee to raise most of the money. It is often easier for a small franchisee to do this if banks know they are running a franchise for a large business because this is likely to mean the business is a viable one and they will be able to generate sales because of the well-known brand name.

Support

For students who struggle with Introduction to franchising:

  • In Teaching session 1, some students may struggle with understanding how franchises work. Suggest that they consider a famous brand they know well and explain that a relatively quick and easy way for that brand to grow into new areas is not to do all the work of setting themselves up, but to get another business to do that for them. Stress that franchising is not a form of ownership (which they have seen in previous lessons) but simply a way of doing business. Some of the advantages on p. 23 of the Student Book may help students understand ‘why’ franchising is used.
  • In Student work 1 (Worksheet 1, Question 2), some students may struggle to choose a business. Suggest they think about a brand they personally would buy from. For example, Liverpool FC may decide to capitalise on their reputation as a world-leading football club in Thailand and franchise out the opportunity to run Liverpool FC coaching lessons. The franchisee would have to be a football fan and take online coaching courses offered by Liverpool. They would also follow standard training drills and then be able to sell kits it and other merchandise and take a fee for each lesson.

  • Ask students: What might be an advantage to the franchisee? (Possible answer: The franchisee does not need to put in as much capital upfront as if it was their own company.)

  1. Entrepreneurs are the business owners, and without these people, businesses would not exist in the private sector. They develop a business idea, invest their own money and start a business.
  2. In a franchising operation, the franchisor owns the franchise. Franchisors receive money when they allow others to take out a franchise and trade under their name.
  3. Innovation
    4. Decision-making Organising Risk-taking
  4. Many small business owners, such as sole traders and franchisees, have unlimited liability. This is very important because it says something about the risk they take. It means the franchisee is liable for all business debts if the business collapses. The franchisee can be forced to meet these debts from personal wealth. It means a person can lose more than was originally invested in the business. In this case, a Husse franchisee could lose more than the original £10 000 paid to the franchisor before trading began.
  5. Taking out a franchise may suit someone who wants to run a business but does not have their own business idea. The Husse franchisor grants the franchisee a licence to trade under the brand name of Husse. In return for £10 000, the Husse franchisee gets various benefits and support. Husse offers benefits such as £2000 of stock for resale, additional materials worth £2000, which includes items such as a Husse tent, signs for a van, catalogues and a two-day in-house training course. Husse franchisees also get an exclusive territory with around 55 000 households. In the first six months, regular money for marketing is provided, and so is support such as advisory services and a development manager. All these benefits are key advantages of operating as a franchisee. There is also less risk when taking out a franchise. Entrepreneurs have to risk their own money to get started. In this case, it costs up to £13 900 to take out a Husse franchise. This is less risky than starting a business alone because the franchisee can trade under an established and successful brand name. In this case, Husse is established in over 50 countries worldwide. Many pet owners will know the ‘tried and trusted’ brand name. This gives the franchisee a head start and reduces the risk of failure – a huge advantage. However, taking out a franchise, such as Husse, has disadvantages. In some franchise agreements, profit is shared with the franchisor. For example, it may be necessary to pay an annual commission in addition to the original fee. There is also a lack of independence. Franchisors often require strict operating conditions. For example, a Husse franchisee may not be allowed to trade outside the agreed territory. Finally, it can be an expensive way to start a new business. It may be cheaper to set up a pet food delivery service without using Husse. To conclude, taking out a franchise may benefit those struggling to come up with their own idea, those who want to reduce some of the risks in business and those who are happy to sacrifice some independence.
